Electrical Principles

T5 is the math-and-physics subelement: the vocabulary and formulas that describe electricity itself, before any of it becomes a radio signal. Nothing here requires more than arithmetic — the goal is just to make sure a formula like E = I × R isn't a magic incantation, but something you could rebuild from scratch if you forgot it. Every worked example below shows the actual arithmetic, step by step.

T5A

Current, voltage & the vocabulary of electricity

Picture electricity as water in a pipe, because the four basic quantities map onto that picture almost exactly:

  • Current is the flow itself — specifically, the flow of electrons through a circuit, the same way water flowing through a pipe is a flow of water molecules. It's measured in amperes (amps), and the term for "how many amps" is current, full stop — not "amperage" or "amps per second."
  • Voltage is the push behind the flow — the electrical equivalent of water pressure. A difference in voltage between two points is specifically what causes electrons to flow between them, the same way water only flows from a high-pressure point to a low-pressure one. Voltage is measured in volts.
  • Power is the rate at which electrical energy is used or delivered — how much work the flow is doing per second, comparable to how much water is moving a waterwheel per second, not just how much water there is. Power is measured in watts.
  • Frequency describes alternating current specifically: the number of times per second the current completes one full cycle. It's measured in hertz (Hz) — one hertz is one cycle per second.

That last term splits current into two families. Direct current (DC) flows in one direction only — a battery is the everyday example. Alternating current (AC) reverses direction on a regular cycle, continuously switching between flowing in a positive direction and a negative direction — household mains power and every RF signal your radio transmits are AC. Both kinds of current are opposed by resistance equally; resistance doesn't care which way the current is flowing, or whether it's DC, AC, or RF.

Whether current flows easily through a material at all depends on how many free electrons that material has — electrons loosely enough bound to their atoms that they can drift from atom to atom under a push from voltage. Metals are good conductors specifically because they have a large supply of free electrons; materials like glass have very few, which is why glass is a good insulator — it blocks current flow rather than carrying it.

Worked example You're told a component "has a lot of free electrons" and asked whether it's likely a conductor or an insulator. Free electrons are exactly what lets current flow — more of them loose and available to drift under a voltage push means less opposition to current, which is the definition of a conductor. So: conductor. (Glass, by contrast, has very few free electrons available, which is why it's used as an insulator.)

Unit conversion & decibels

Electronics uses one consistent set of metric prefixes to avoid writing endless zeros, and the pool expects you to convert between them fluently in both directions:

Prefix Multiplier Example
giga (G)×1,000,000,0002.425 GHz
mega (M)×1,000,0001500 kHz = 1.5 MHz
kilo (k)×1,0001 kilovolt = 1,000 volts
milli (m)÷1,000500 milliwatts = 0.5 watts
micro (µ)÷1,000,0001 microvolt = 0.000001 volts
pico (p)÷1,000,000,0001,000,000 picofarads = 1 microfarad

The mechanical trick is simple: to convert from a bigger unit to a smaller one (kilo to milli, say), you multiply and the number gets bigger; to go from a smaller unit to a bigger one, you divide and the number gets smaller. Moving one step in the table (kilo→base, or base→milli) shifts the decimal point three places; moving from mega down to milli shifts it nine places.

Worked example How many milliamperes is 1.5 amperes? "Milli" means ÷1,000, so going the other way — from the base unit to milli — means multiplying by 1,000: 1.5 × 1,000 = 1,500 milliamperes. Same logic in reverse: 3,000 milliamperes ÷ 1,000 = 3 amperes.

Frequency prefixes work identically. 1,500,000 hertz ÷ 1,000 = 1,500,000 hertz expressed as 1,500 kHz (dividing by 1,000 to go from hertz to kilohertz); the same value divided by 1,000 again is 1.5 MHz. And 28,400 kHz ÷ 1,000 = 28.400 MHz — recognizable as a frequency inside Technician's 10-meter phone segment from T1B.

Decibels: measuring a ratio, not an amount

A decibel (dB) doesn't measure power directly — it measures the ratio between two power levels, on a logarithmic scale, because human perception of signal strength (and the range of power levels radios deal with) is itself closer to logarithmic than linear. Three reference numbers cover everything the exam asks:

  • +3 dB — power has doubled.
  • −3 dB — power has been cut in half.
  • 10 dB — power has changed by a factor of 10× (up for +10 dB, down for −10 dB).

Those three combine by simple addition, because decibels are logarithmic: a change that's "double, then double again" is +3 dB + 3 dB = +6 dB for a 4× power increase; "10×, then double" is 10 dB + 3 dB = 13 dB for a 20× increase.

Worked example Power increases from 5 watts to 10 watts — what's the change in dB? 10 ÷ 5 = 2, so the power exactly doubled. Doubling power is always +3 dB, regardless of the starting wattage.
Worked example Power decreases from 12 watts to 3 watts. 12 ÷ 3 = 4, and a 4× decrease is "cut in half, then cut in half again": −3 dB + (−3 dB) = −6 dB.
Worked example Power increases from 20 watts to 200 watts. 200 ÷ 20 = 10 exactly, a straight 10× increase, so the answer is +10 dB — no need to break it into doubling steps when the ratio is a clean power of ten.

T5C

Capacitance, inductance & impedance

Two components store electrical energy without spending it as heat, each in a different kind of field:

Capacitance

The ability to store energy in an electric field. A capacitor is typically two conductive plates separated by an insulator; apply voltage and charge builds up on the plates. Capacitance is measured in farads (commonly microfarads or picofarads, since a full farad is enormous for small-signal circuits).

Inductance

The ability to store energy in a magnetic field. An inductor is typically a coil of wire; current flowing through it builds up a magnetic field around the coil. Inductance is measured in henrys.

Reactance is the opposition capacitors and inductors present to AC current specifically — unlike plain resistance, reactance depends on frequency, which is exactly why capacitors and inductors are the building blocks of filters and tuning circuits (a fact you'll meet again when antenna tuners come up in T9). Combine resistance and reactance together in an AC circuit and you get impedance — the total opposition to AC current flow, measured in ohms, the same unit as plain resistance. Impedance is the number that matters for any AC or RF circuit; resistance alone is only the DC piece of that picture.

Radio frequency (RF) itself just refers to the range of alternating-current frequencies that can be radiated usefully as radio waves — the frequencies your transmitter, feed line, and antenna are all designed around, as opposed to, say, 60 Hz household AC. Two abbreviations from that world are worth memorizing exactly as written, since the pool tests the capitalization: kHz for kilohertz and MHz for megahertz.

DC power: the one formula this group actually tests numerically

In a DC circuit, power equals voltage times current:

DC power formula

P = I × E

P = power (watts), I = current (amps), E = voltage (volts)

Worked example A 12-volt DC circuit draws 2.5 amperes. How much power is that? P = I × E = 2.5 × 12 = 30 watts.
Worked example 13.8 volts DC (a typical mobile radio's supply) at 10 amperes: P = I × E = 10 × 13.8 = 138 watts.
Worked example You need to deliver 120 watts at 12 volts — how much current does that take? Rearrange the formula to solve for current: I = P ÷ E = 120 ÷ 12 = 10 amperes.

T5D

Ohm's Law & series/parallel circuits

Ohm's Law ties voltage, current, and resistance together in a DC circuit. It's one relationship, but the pool tests all three ways of rearranging it, so it's worth being able to solve for whichever quantity you're missing:

Solve for current

I = E ÷ R

Solve for voltage

E = I × R

Solve for resistance

R = E ÷ I

Legend

E = voltage (volts), I = current (amps), R = resistance (ohms)

A memory trick: cover up the quantity you're solving for in the triangle "E over I, R" (E on top, I and R on the bottom) and whatever's left tells you whether to multiply or divide — cover E and you're left with I next to R, meaning multiply (E = I × R); cover I or R and you're left with E over the other one, meaning divide. Or skip memorizing the triangle entirely and just play with the relationship directly:

Worked example — solving for resistance A circuit draws 3 amperes when connected to 90 volts. What's the resistance? R = E ÷ I = 90 ÷ 3 = 30 ohms.
Worked example — solving for current 120 volts applied across 80 ohms of resistance. I = E ÷ R = 120 ÷ 80 = 1.5 amperes.
Worked example — solving for voltage A current of 2 amperes flows through a 10-ohm resistor. What's the voltage across it? E = I × R = 2 × 10 = 20 volts.

Series vs. parallel: what stays the same

The pool's series/parallel questions boil down to one fact each, and they're mirror images of each other:

  • In a series circuit — components chained one after another, a single loop — there's only one path for electrons to take, so the current is the same everywhere in the loop. Think of a single-lane road: every car that passes one point passes every other point too.
  • In a parallel circuit — components each connected directly across the same two points — every component sees the identical voltage, because they all share the same two connection points, even though the current can split up and take different amounts through each branch. Think of several parallel lanes all starting and ending at the same intersections: the "pressure" pushing traffic is the same on every lane, but each lane can carry a different amount of traffic.
Worked example Two resistors are wired in parallel across a 12-volt battery. Does each resistor see 12 volts, or does the voltage split between them the way it would in series? Parallel means both components connect across the exact same two points — so both see the full 12 volts. (If they were wired in series instead, it would be the current that's identical through both, not the voltage.)

Operating Procedures

T1 was the law. T2 is the etiquette — the actual mechanics of making a contact: how to call, how to answer, how a repeater's split works, and how the public-service side of the hobby (nets, emergency traffic) is organized. None of this is arbitrary tradition; each convention below solves a specific, practical problem.

T2A

Calling, answering & band plans

Two operating modes underlie everything in this subelement. In simplex operation, both stations transmit and receive on the same single frequency — you hear the other station exactly where you're transmitting. A repeater instead listens on one frequency and retransmits on another (more on that in T2B); a station transmitting and receiving on two different frequencies simultaneously like that is full duplex, not simplex. Most handheld and mobile VHF/UHF operating — and every HF phone contact you'll make — is simplex.

Calling CQ is how you ask "is anyone listening?" when you're not answering someone specific. The conventional pattern: say "CQ" a few times, then "this is," then your call sign, then pause and actually listen before repeating. CQ itself is simply the procedural signal for calling any station — it isn't an abbreviation for anything, it's just the internationally recognized "I'm available, who's out there" call. If you hear someone else's CQ and want to answer, the order matters: say their call sign first, then yours — so the station you're calling knows immediately that the transmission is meant for them, before they have to figure out whose voice they're hearing.

On a repeater, if you already know the call sign of the station you want to reach, the pattern flips slightly from a cold CQ: say their call sign, then identify with your own. And if you just want to let others know you're around and open to a contact — without directing it at anyone specific — say your own call sign followed by the word "listening." That's the customary way to invite a call on a repeater without a full CQ.

Worked example You want to make a casual contact on a repeater and don't have anyone specific in mind. You key up and say: "KJ7ABC listening." A minute later another station replies "KJ7ABC, this is W1XYZ." Notice the pattern holds both directions: you announced availability with your own call sign, and the responding station led with your call sign before their own, so you'd know immediately the call was meant for you.

Outside of calling procedure, a band plan is a voluntary gentleman's-agreement layer on top of the FCC's actual privileges (which are hard legal limits from T1B) — a plan, usually coordinated by amateurs themselves, for which slice of a band is customarily used for which mode or activity (say, FM simplex vs. weak-signal CW vs. satellite work within the same band). Nothing in a band plan is individually enforceable by the FCC the way §97.301 privileges are — but ignoring it is a fast way to cause interference to whoever expected that slice to be used a particular way. Simplex channels specifically are called out in VHF/UHF band plans for a practical reason covered next in T2B: keeping local, in-range contacts off repeaters entirely.

T2B

Repeaters, tones & digital systems

A repeater listens on one frequency and simultaneously retransmits on another, so two stations that can't hear each other directly can both hear the repeater. The repeater offset (or "split") is simply the gap between those two frequencies. It's a fixed convention per band, not something you calculate fresh each time: a common 2-meter offset is ±600 kHz, while 70 centimeters commonly uses ±5 MHz. That's why your radio just needs to know "positive split" or "negative split" plus the offset convention for the band, not two arbitrary frequencies. The reverse function on a VHF/UHF transceiver temporarily swaps this — it lets you listen on the repeater's input (receive) frequency instead of its output, which is useful for checking whether you can hear the other station directly, without the repeater's help.

Worked example You can hear a repeater's output clearly, but nothing you transmit gets repeated back. What's wrong? Several things could independently cause this: your transceiver's offset might not match the repeater's split, you might be using the wrong CTCSS tone (below), or the wrong DCS code. The symptom "I can hear it but can't access it" doesn't pin down which one — you have to check all three.

A single repeater only covers the area its own antenna site can reach. A linked repeater network extends that by wiring multiple repeaters together (often over the internet or a dedicated RF link) so that whatever one repeater receives, every other repeater in the network retransmits too — you're not just talking through one repeater anymore, you're talking through all of them at once, which is how a signal from one city can be heard clearly in another with no direct RF path between them at all.

Two access-tone systems gate whether a repeater (or your own squelch) opens up for a signal:

  • CTCSS (Continuous Tone-Coded Squelch System) rides a single sub-audible tone underneath your voice audio the whole time you transmit. The repeater's receiver is listening for that specific tone; if it's present, the squelch opens. It's called "sub-audible" because it sits below the frequency range your ear (and the repeater's audio filtering) actually passes through as sound — you don't hear it, but the receiver's tone decoder does.
  • DTMF (Dual-Tone Multi-Frequency) is the opposite kind of signal: short bursts, not continuous, and each keypress sends two simultaneous tones at once (the same system your phone's touch-tone keypad uses). It's used for one-off commands — dialing an autopatch, or controlling certain repeater/DMR functions — not for holding a squelch open.

A few plain mechanical facts about FM audio worth knowing here: talking too loudly into an FM transceiver's microphone — overdriving it — is what causes your transmitted audio to distort or drop out on voice peaks, since the deviation is being pushed past what the receiver's filters cleanly pass. And a squelch circuit's whole job is to mute your receiver's audio output whenever no signal is present, so you don't have to listen to constant background hiss between transmissions.

Digital voice systems: DMR, D-STAR, and talkgroups

Digital repeater systems like DMR replace analog CTCSS/DTMF signaling with their own mechanisms. A talkgroup is how DMR organizes traffic into separate logical channels sharing the same repeater hardware — think of it as a filter so you only hear the conversations you actually want, even though many different groups might be using the same physical repeater. You join one by programming your radio with that talkgroup's specific ID or code — there's no registration process with a person or a Frequency Coordinator involved. The full set of talkgroups, repeaters, and access codes your radio knows about comes from its code plug, a configuration file you load onto the radio (often distributed by the local DMR network so everyone's radio matches).

DMR also has its own gatekeeping mechanism separate from talkgroups: the color code. It looks like CTCSS's digital cousin, but it isn't a privacy or grouping feature — it's an access code (numbered 0–15) that has to be programmed into your radio to match the repeater before the repeater will accept your transmission at all, the same basic gatekeeping role CTCSS plays for analog FM, just implemented digitally.

D-STAR is a separate digital voice protocol with its own quirk worth knowing: because your identification is carried digitally as part of the signal itself, a D-STAR radio requires your call sign to be programmed in before it will transmit at all — there's no separate voice identification step layered on top the way there is on analog FM.

One everyday courtesy fact that predates all the digital systems: if two stations transmitting on the same simplex frequency start interfering with each other, there's no FCC-enforced "first come, first served" rule at play the way there is between primary and secondary allocations in T1B — the expected practice is simply that the two stations negotiate who moves.

That kind of negotiation is exactly what Q signals exist for — a small set of three-letter shorthand codes, inherited from landline telegraph and CW operating, that pack a whole operational question or statement into three letters so it's faster to send than spelling it out. Two show up on the exam: QRM means you're receiving interference from other stations (not from weather or static — specifically man-made signal interference), and QSY means changing frequency. Saying "QSY to 146.52" is a compact way of saying "let's move this conversation to 146.52 MHz" — the same negotiation this section just described, expressed in the shorthand hams actually use on the air.

T2C

Public service, nets & emergency communications

One rule anchors this whole group: FCC Part 97 rules always apply to an amateur station's operation (§97.103(a)) — there's no special "emergency mode" that suspends them. What emergencies do unlock is a narrow, specific exception already previewed in T1D: a control operator may transmit outside their license class's normal frequency privileges, but only in situations involving the immediate safety of human life or protection of property — not merely because an organized net or emergency exercise is underway.

Two volunteer organizations get confused with each other constantly, and the pool tests the distinction directly:

ARES

Amateur Radio Emergency Service — hams who've voluntarily registered their qualifications and equipment for public-service duty. No outside certification required beyond your amateur license.

RACES

Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service — an FCC Part 97 service specifically for civil defense communications during national emergencies. Its operators need certification by a civil defense agency, on top of an amateur license — the one thing ARES, MARS, and SKYWARN don't require.

An organized net is a group of stations meeting on a set frequency at a set time, run by a Net Control Station whose job is to call the net to order and direct who transmits when — not to check anyone's license validity, and not to set the meeting time itself (that's usually decided in advance by whoever organizes the net). Standard net etiquette follows directly from that: unless you're reporting an actual emergency, you transmit only when Net Control directs you to, not whenever you feel like jumping in.

Traffic, in net terminology, just means formal messages being relayed between stations — not a count of check-ins, not a measure of net activity. The standard written form for a formal message is a radiogram. Two of its fields get tested directly because their names are misleading if you guess from context: the preamble is the routing/tracking information at the top (not the sender's email or the recipient's address), and the check is simply a count of the words (or word-equivalents) in the message text — a lightweight integrity check, so a station relaying the message can confirm nothing got dropped in transit, the same way a checksum works on a data file.

Worked example A message contains an unusual proper name that's easy to mishear over voice. What do you do? Spell it out using a standard phonetic alphabet ("Alpha Bravo Charlie…") — the same tool T1A mentioned as encouraged-not-required for routine identification becomes the actual fix here, precisely because it exists to remove ambiguity in exactly this kind of situation.

One more system worth knowing by name: Winlink relays messages to and from ordinary internet email addresses using amateur call signs as the routing identity — useful when a station has an RF link but no direct internet access.

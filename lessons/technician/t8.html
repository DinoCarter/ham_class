T8A

FM & SSB characteristics, and mode bandwidth

SSB (single sideband) is technically a form of amplitude modulation: an AM signal actually contains a carrier plus two mirror-image sidebands carrying the same information, so SSB just removes the carrier and one of those redundant sidebands, transmitting only what's actually needed. By convention, upper sideband (USB) is the one used for SSB voice on 10-meter HF, and on every VHF/UHF band — you don't need to choose, your radio defaults to it. FM (or PM) is what's normally used for VHF/UHF voice repeaters and for VHF packet radio; SSB, by contrast, is the voice mode of choice for long-distance, weak-signal VHF/UHF contacts, because (as below) it packs into much less bandwidth than FM does for the same information.

Bandwidth is just how much spectrum a signal actually occupies, and it varies enormously by mode — mostly because of how much information per second each mode is actually trying to send:

Mode Typical bandwidth Why
CW≈150 HzNarrowest of all — just on/off keying, no audio to carry
SSB voice≈3 kHzOne sideband, roughly matching voice audio bandwidth
FM voice (VHF repeater)10–15 kHzDeliberately spread wider — trades spectrum for noise immunity
Fast-scan (NTSC) TV≈6 MHzA full moving picture needs vastly more information per second
Worked example How many separate CW signals fit in the same slice of spectrum as a single FM voice repeater signal? At roughly 150 Hz per CW signal versus 10–15 kHz for one FM voice signal, that's on the order of 60–100 CW signals in the same space — and even a single 3 kHz SSB voice signal only uses a fraction of that FM slice. That's the concrete reason CW gets the special "legal anywhere" privilege back in T1B, and why busy contest weekends are packed wall-to-wall with CW: it's simply the most spectrum-efficient way to fit in more signals.

One real operational downside of FM versus SSB: on FM, if two stations transmit on the same frequency at once, a receiver locks onto whichever is stronger and completely suppresses the weaker one — a behavior sometimes called the capture effect — so only one signal can be received at a time even if a weaker second station is also present. SSB, being amplitude-based rather than a hard either/or, doesn't behave that way.

T8B

Amateur satellite operation

A satellite beacon is a transmission from the satellite itself that carries the health and status of the spacecraft — not a general propagation beacon like T1A's HF ones, but telemetry about the satellite. Reception of that telemetry is open to anyone — there's no special authorization needed, which lines up with T1D's ban on encoding messages to hide their meaning: amateur satellite telemetry is meant to be public.

Satellite tracking software needs real orbital data to work: its inputs are the Keplerian elements — a standard, regularly-updated set of numbers describing a satellite's orbit shape, tilt, and position, published by organizations that track every object in orbit. Given those, a tracking program can output everything an operator needs for a pass: a real-time ground-track map, the timing/azimuth/elevation of the pass's start, peak, and end, and the apparent frequency of the satellite's transmission accounting for Doppler shift.

Doppler shift is an observed change in received frequency caused by relative motion between the satellite and your station — the same effect as a passing siren changing pitch. As a satellite rises toward you, its signal reads slightly higher than its nominal frequency; right at the moment of closest approach (straight overhead, for a pass that goes directly over you) the relative motion toward/away from you is momentarily zero, so it reads right at nominal; as it recedes toward the far horizon, the signal reads slightly lower.

Worked example You're listening to a low earth orbit satellite's downlink. As it clears your horizon and rises (AOS), the signal sounds slightly higher-pitched than its published frequency; a few minutes later, as it drops back toward the opposite horizon (LOS), it sounds slightly lower. That drift is exactly why serious satellite operators re-tune every minute or so during a pass instead of setting the dial once and leaving it — the Doppler shift during a single pass can move the signal enough to drift out of a narrow filter.

A few more named vocabulary items complete the picture. Spin fading — a distinct effect from Doppler shift — is caused by rotation of the satellite and its antennas: as the spacecraft tumbles, its antenna pattern sweeps past Earth rhythmically, so signal strength rises and falls even though the frequency isn't changing. LEO (Low Earth Orbit) describes satellites with an orbital period of around 100 minutes — close enough to Earth that they circle it quickly, which is also why a single pass only lasts several minutes. "U/V mode" describes a satellite whose uplink is on the 70-centimeter band and downlink is on the 2-meter band. And excessive uplink power into a linear transponder satellite doesn't damage the bird — its real-world impact is blocking access for other users, since the whole transponder is a shared resource and one overly strong signal desensitizes it for everyone riding through it at once. The practical way to judge your own uplink power is comparing your downlink signal strength to the beacon's: if it's about the same, your power is in the right range — neither too weak to be heard nor loud enough to be hogging the transponder.

T8C

Operating activities

Radio direction finding (RDF) uses directional antennas and signal-strength comparisons to locate a transmitter — whether that's a "hidden transmitter hunt" run for fun, or tracking down a genuine source of noise or intentional jamming. A directional antenna is the one piece of gear that actually matters for a hunt like that.

Contesting is the operating activity built around contacting as many stations as possible in a fixed period of time. Good contest practice follows directly from that goal: send only the minimum information needed for identification and the contest exchange — padding a transmission with extra pleasantries, or working the same station twice "just to be sure," costs everyone time that could go to another contact.

A grid locator is a letter-number code assigned to a geographic location, exchanged constantly in VHF/UHF weak-signal work, satellite operating, and contest logs as compact shorthand for "roughly where I am," without reading out a full address over the air.

Three related systems link amateur radio to the internet, and it's worth keeping their layers straight:

VoIP

The general underlying technology: delivering voice communications over the internet using digital techniques. Not amateur-specific by itself.

IRLP & EchoLink

Two specific amateur systems built on VoIP-style technology, each connecting repeaters/stations together over the internet.

On the air, you don't type an address for either one: IRLP node access is done with DTMF tones keyed in on your own radio. EchoLink is slightly different in an important way — it can let a station transmit through a repeater without a radio initiating the connection at all (a computer or phone app can reach the system directly) — which is exactly why EchoLink requires you to register your call sign and provide proof of license before you can use it: there's no radio-side license check happening automatically the way there normally would be. An amateur gateway station is the general term for a station that connects other amateur stations to the internet, whichever specific system (IRLP, EchoLink, or another) it's running.

T8D

Non-voice & digital communications

CW is, quite simply, another name for a Morse code transmission — nothing more exotic than that, and worth restating plainly since T8 tests it as a bare definition question. On the image side, NTSC is the specific analog fast-scan color TV signal standard behind the "image" mode category from T1B — it's what gives fast-scan amateur TV its roughly 6 MHz bandwidth from the T8A table, in contrast to SSTV's narrow, still-image-only bandwidth.

Packet radio breaks data into discrete chunks, each with three things attached: a header (containing the call sign of the station the data is addressed to), a checksum (lets the receiving station detect whether the packet arrived corrupted), and ARQ (automatic repeat request) — if the checksum reveals an error, the receiving station requests that specific packet be resent, rather than the operator having to notice and fix anything manually.

Worked example A packet destined for KI7ABC hits a burst of noise partway through transmission that corrupts a few bits. The receiving station's checksum calculation won't match what it expects, so it detects the error and — via ARQ — automatically asks the sending station to resend just that packet. Neither operator has to notice anything went wrong; the protocol handles it.

PSK (Phase Shift Keying) is a digital mode that encodes information by shifting a carrier's phase, rather than switching it fully on and off (like CW) or changing its frequency — PSK31 is the common ham example, popular for combining narrow bandwidth with solid weak-signal performance. FT8, first mentioned back in T4A as a mode you interface to a computer over ordinary audio connections, is a digital mode specifically capable of decoding signals well below what's audible by ear — low signal-to-noise-ratio operation is its whole selling point.

APRS (Automatic Packet Reporting System) automatically transmits real-time information — GPS position, short text messages, and weather data are all typical APRS payloads — and its signature application is providing real-time tactical digital communications together with a map showing where every reporting station currently is.

DMR, already introduced back in T2B and T4B for its talkgroups and code plugs, has one more specific technical trick worth knowing: it time-multiplexes two separate digital voice conversations onto a single 12.5 kHz-wide repeater channel — the two conversations take turns in rapid alternating time slices, sharing one channel the way two conversations might share one lane by taking quick turns, rather than needing two separate channels.

The WSJT-X software suite (which includes FT8) supports a cluster of specialized weak-signal digital activities: EME (Earth-Moon-Earth, bouncing a signal off the Moon and back), weak-signal propagation beacons, and meteor scatter (the same brief-ionized-trail propagation mode from T3C) — all real, specialized techniques for pulling contacts out of very weak signals, not separate unrelated software features.

Finally, an amateur radio mesh network is a data network built from commercially available Wi-Fi equipment running modified firmware on amateur bands — ordinary consumer hardware repurposed and reflashed specifically for ham use, not custom-built radio gear.

Radio Wave Propagation

T1 and T2 were about what you're allowed to do and how to do it politely. T3 is physics: what a radio wave actually is, and why it sometimes travels 10 feet and sometimes 10,000 miles. Nothing here is a regulation — it's the underlying science the pool expects you to understand well enough to predict when a contact will and won't work.

T3A

Why signals behave the way they do

Start with a puzzling everyday observation: on VHF, moving your antenna just a few feet can make a weak signal disappear entirely, or suddenly come in strong. The cause is multipath — your receiver isn't hearing just one copy of the signal, it's hearing the direct path plus reflections off the ground, buildings, hills, even passing vehicles, all arriving at slightly different times. Those copies can add together in phase (reinforcing each other) or arrive out of phase (partially or fully cancelling each other), and which one happens depends on the exact path lengths involved — which is why moving your antenna by a fraction of a wavelength changes everything. The same mechanism explains two other symptoms directly: irregular fading on ionospheric HF signals (multiple sky-wave paths combining unpredictably), and picket fencing — the rapid flutter you hear on a mobile FM signal as a moving vehicle sweeps continuously through alternating zones of reinforcement and cancellation. On digital data links, that same unpredictable path-combining doesn't sound like anything — it just shows up as a higher bit error rate, since multipath's actual effect is corrupting the signal's timing and amplitude, not amplifying or slowing it in any way you could "correct" by transmitting faster or slower.

Polarization — the orientation of the wave's electric field — matters because transmit and receive antennas generally need to match for the strongest signal. For long-distance VHF/UHF CW and SSB contacts, the convention is horizontal polarization (most repeaters, by contrast, use vertical, which is why an HT's vertical "rubber duck" antenna works fine there but poorly for weak-signal horizontal work). Get the polarization of the two stations badly mismatched — cross-polarized, say vertical talking to horizontal — and received signal strength drops, sometimes severely, even though everything else about the path is fine.

Worked example You're trying to make a weak-signal SSB contact on 2 meters with a station 80 miles away, using a horizontally polarized Yagi. Your neighbor, working a local repeater 5 miles off with a vertical antenna, has no trouble at all. Different jobs, different polarization conventions: horizontal for long-distance VHF/UHF SSB and CW, vertical for local FM/repeater work — matching the convention that the other stations you're trying to reach are actually using.

Two mechanical facts about how obstacles and weather interact with signals, at opposite ends of the spectrum: vegetation (trees, foliage) absorbs UHF and microwave signals, degrading weak-signal reception noticeably — a UHF handheld in dense woods performs worse than the same radio in the open, even with an identical path distance. At the very top of the amateur spectrum, precipitation specifically (not wind, not cold, not pressure) is what degrades microwave range, through a mechanism called rain fade — the water droplets themselves absorb and scatter those short wavelengths. Down at 10 and 6 meters, by contrast, fog and rain have little effect — those wavelengths are long enough that small water droplets barely interact with the signal at all. And one purely geometric point worth remembering when a direct path is blocked by a building or hill: a directional antenna can sometimes still reach a distant repeater by deliberately aiming at a surface that reflects the signal toward it, turning multipath from a nuisance into a tool.

Last, a fact that resolves an intuitive worry about HF skywave contacts: since the ionosphere reflects signals in a way that scrambles their polarization (they arrive elliptically polarized after the trip), it doesn't matter whether your HF antenna is vertical or horizontal for making the contact — either works, because by the time the signal comes back down neither the original transmit polarization nor a specific matching receive polarization is preserved.

T3B

What a radio wave actually is

A radio wave has exactly two components: an electric field and a magnetic field, oscillating together and always at right angles to each other (and to the direction the wave is travelling). Polarization, from T3A, is defined specifically as the orientation of the electric field — the magnetic field is always along for the ride at 90° to it, so there's no separate "magnetic polarization" to track.

In free space (a vacuum, and for practical purposes, air), every radio wave travels at the same speed regardless of frequency: the speed of light, approximately 300,000,000 meters per second. Microwaves, UHF, VHF, and HF all travel at that identical velocity — frequency doesn't speed anything up or slow anything down in free space. What frequency does change is wavelength, and the relationship is inverse: as frequency goes up, wavelength gets shorter, because the same velocity has to fit more oscillation cycles into the same one second.

That relationship reduces to one formula, derived directly from (speed of light) ÷ frequency, with the units simplified for convenience:

Wavelength formula

wavelength (meters) = 300 ÷ frequency (MHz)

Worked example Technician's 10-meter band sits around 28 MHz. Check the name against the formula: 300 ÷ 28 ≈ 10.7 meters — close enough to "10 meters" that the band name is just the rounded wavelength, not an arbitrary label. Do the same check on the 2-meter band at 146 MHz: 300 ÷ 146 ≈ 2.05 meters. This is exactly why amateur bands get identified by wavelength in addition to frequency — "10 meters" and "28 MHz" are two names for the same physical thing.

T3C

How signals get from here to there

The single biggest propagation fact to internalize: HF gets long-distance help from the ionosphere routinely; VHF and UHF generally don't. The ionosphere is a layered, ionized region of the upper atmosphere (roughly 50–600 km up) that can bend HF waves back down to Earth — that's what makes working a station a thousand miles away on 10 meters unremarkable, while a UHF simplex signal is usually only heard out to its radio horizon (which extends slightly past the visual horizon, because the atmosphere refracts radio waves a small amount as they skim along the curve of the Earth — but that's a modest bonus, not ionospheric skip). VHF/UHF signals mostly pass straight through the ionosphere into space rather than reflecting off it.

That said, VHF/UHF has its own set of occasional long-distance tricks, each with a distinct mechanism and a distinct "signature" you can learn to recognize:

Sporadic E

Dense, patchy clouds of ionization in the E layer — much lower than the F layer — briefly reflect signals well above what the F layer normally handles. The propagation mode most responsible for surprise strong openings on 10, 6, and 2 meters.

Tropospheric ducting

A temperature inversion in the lower atmosphere traps and guides VHF/UHF signals in a duct, regularly reaching on the order of 300 miles — well past the normal radio horizon, with no ionosphere involved at all.

Meteor scatter

Signals bounce off the brief ionized trail a meteor leaves burning up in the atmosphere. The trail lasts a fraction of a second to a few seconds, so contacts use short, fast transmissions timed to that window — 6 meters is the band best suited to it.

Auroral backscatter

Signals reflect off ionization in the aurora itself rather than travelling a direct path. The turbulent, shifting reflector gives received signals a characteristic distorted, raspy sound — the signature that gives this mode away on the air.

One more non-ionospheric, non-tropospheric mechanism worth knowing: knife-edge diffraction — radio waves bend slightly around the sharp edge of an obstruction (a ridgeline, a building edge), letting a signal reach a receiver with no true line-of-sight path to the transmitter at all.

Worked example You're on 6 meters and suddenly start hearing stations 1,200 miles away with strong, clean signals for about twenty minutes, then nothing. What happened? Too far for tropospheric ducting (typically ~300 miles) and too clean/sustained for meteor scatter (which comes in short bursts) — this pattern, distance, and band are the classic signature of sporadic E.

Finally, the F-region skip that makes 10-meter long-distance contacts possible depends heavily on solar activity and time of day: it's generally best from dawn to shortly after sunset, during periods of high sunspot activity — sunlight drives the ionization the F region needs, and once the sun sets that ionization starts to decay. During a sunspot cycle's peak, both 6 and 10 meters can get F-region long-distance openings; the higher UHF/microwave bands are still too high in frequency for the F region to bend back down, sunspot cycle or not.

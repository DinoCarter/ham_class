Amateur Radio Practices

T4 is the hands-on subelement: how a station actually gets wired together (T4A), and how you drive the controls on the front panel once it's built (T4B). Both groups are full of small, concrete facts — the kind you either know from having done it, or have to learn deliberately before the exam.

T4A

Setting up a station

A typical 50-watt-output mobile FM transceiver expects a power supply rated 13.8 volts at 12 amperes — 13.8 V because that's the nominal voltage of a vehicle's charging system (a fully charged 12-volt lead-acid battery actually sits closer to 13.8 V under charge), and 12 A because RF output power is only part of what the radio draws; the rest goes to inefficiency in the amplifier stage and to the receiver/control circuitry. That current draw is also why DC power wiring uses short, heavy-gauge wire: a thin or long run has enough resistance that it drops a meaningful chunk of that voltage before it reaches the radio, especially right when you key up and current spikes — not because of any RF concern, just plain Ohm's-law voltage drop under load. The negative return for a mobile installation should connect directly at the vehicle's 12-volt battery chassis ground — not to whatever metal happens to be nearby — so that return current has a low-resistance, predictable path back to the source instead of hunting through the car's body panels.

Worked example You want to know how long a 10 amp-hour battery will run a radio that draws an average of 2 amps. Divide: 10 Ah ÷ 2 A = 5 hours. That's the whole calculation — battery ampere-hour rating divided by the equipment's average current draw, not its peak transmit current, since most operating time on voice is spent receiving or between transmissions.

Two meters, two different jobs. An RF power meter belongs in the feed line, between the transmitter and the antenna — it's measuring the RF actually headed to the antenna, so it has to physically sit in that path. An SWR meter measures a different thing entirely: how well the antenna system is matched to the transmitter, which determines how much of your power radiates versus reflects back. When picking an accessory SWR meter, the detail that actually matters is whether it's rated for the frequency and power level you'll be measuring at — an SWR meter built for HF power levels won't necessarily read correctly at 2 meters, and one built for QRP won't survive full legal-limit power.

Bonding is the practice of tying every metal part of a station — chassis, tower, ground rods — together electrically so nothing floats at a different RF potential from anything else, cutting down on stray RF and reducing shock and fire risk. The preferred conductor for RF bonding is flat copper strap, not round wire — strap has much more surface area for the same cross-section, and RF current concentrates near a conductor's surface (the "skin effect"), so more surface area means lower impedance exactly where it counts at radio frequencies.

Digital modes and the computer-radio link

Running a mode like FT8 just means connecting your transceiver's audio in and out to your computer's audio in and out, with software (not a separate hardware "FT8 converter") doing the encoding and decoding. A full computer-radio digital interface typically carries exactly three signals: receive audio (radio → computer), transmit audio (computer → radio), and transmitter keying (so the software can key the radio into transmit without you touching a push-to-talk button). Concretely, that means the computer's "line in" connects to the transceiver's speaker connector (receive audio flowing into the computer) — not to push-to-talk, which is a separate keying line entirely.

A digital mode hotspot is a small personal device that bridges a nearby handheld to a digital voice or data network, usually over the internet — it's how a low-power radio can reach a DMR/D-STAR talkgroup or reflector without a full-size repeater anywhere in range. And an electronic keyer is a device that assists sending Morse code manually — it takes paddle motions from the operator and turns them into correctly timed dits and dahs, rather than requiring every individual element to be tapped out by hand on a straight key.

T4B

Operating controls

You can enter a transceiver's operating frequency two ways: the keypad (typing a specific frequency directly) or the VFO knob (Variable Frequency Oscillator — tuning continuously by hand). Both are normal, direct methods of frequency entry; CTCSS/DTMF encoders and Automatic Frequency Control do other jobs entirely and don't set your operating frequency.

Two controls get misused in ways that produce distorted audio, for two different reasons: pushing microphone gain too high on SSB overdrives the audio stage and distorts your transmitted signal, the voice-mode equivalent of the FM over-deviation problem from T2B. Separately, an FM signal that's received slightly off frequency comes out distorted too — not quieter, not shifted in pitch uniformly, but distorted — because FM demodulation depends on tracking the carrier's frequency swings accurately, and a receiver tuned even a little off that carrier decodes those swings incorrectly.

Squelch normally mutes your receiver between transmissions so you don't hear constant hiss. To pull in a weak FM signal that's having trouble holding the squelch open, the fix is to set the squelch threshold low enough that receiver audio is on all the time — effectively opening the gate all the way so nothing gets muted, at the cost of hearing background noise between transmissions. The scanning function does something unrelated: it tunes through a range of frequencies automatically, checking each one for activity, so you don't have to step through channels by hand.

If a station replying to your SSB CQ sounds slightly too high- or low-pitched, that's a small frequency mismatch between your receiver and their transmitter — the fix is the RIT or Clarifier control (Receiver Incremental Tuning), which nudges your receive frequency without moving your transmit frequency, so you can correct the pitch without accidentally drifting off the frequency the other station is listening on.

Multimode transceivers offer a choice of receiver filter bandwidths for a concrete reason: matching the filter to the mode reduces noise and interference by excluding everything outside the bandwidth the signal actually needs. Too narrow, and you clip the signal itself; too wide, and you let in noise and adjacent-signal interference unnecessarily. For SSB voice specifically, 2400 Hz gives the best signal-to-noise ratio among the common choices — narrow enough to exclude a good deal of noise, wide enough to pass the range of frequencies SSB voice actually needs (500 Hz and 1000 Hz are too narrow for clear voice; 5000 Hz passes more noise than necessary).

Worked example You're switching between working weak-signal CW and casual SSB ragchews on the same transceiver. Why not just leave the filter on the widest setting for everything? On CW, a wide SSB-width filter passes far more noise than a narrow CW filter would, for no benefit — a CW signal only occupies a tiny slice of spectrum. Matching filter bandwidth to mode is exactly this trade-off, repeated for every mode you operate.

Two digital-voice-specific facts round out this group, both call-backs to T2B: a DMR radio is switched to a specific talkgroup by entering that group's identification code — not by picking a CTCSS tone or inserting a physical code plug adapter, since the code plug is software already loaded on the radio, not a hardware key. And a D-STAR transceiver specifically must have your call sign programmed into it before it will transmit at all, since D-STAR carries identification digitally as part of the signal rather than relying on you to speak it.

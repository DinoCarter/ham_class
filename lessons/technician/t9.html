Antennas and Feed Lines

T5 covered the electrical theory; T3 covered how a wave travels once it's launched. T9 is the hardware in between: the antenna that turns your transmitter's power into a radio wave (and vice versa on receive), and the feed line that carries power between the radio and the antenna. Two groups: T9A is about antennas themselves, T9B is about getting power to them without losing too much of it along the way.

T9A Antenna types, polarization & gain T9B Feed lines, SWR & tuners

T9A

Antenna types, polarization & gain

The simplest practical antenna is a dipole: a straight wire split in the middle, fed at that center point, with each half roughly a quarter-wavelength long (a "half-wave dipole" overall). Its radiation pattern isn't uniform — a half-wave dipole radiates its strongest signal broadside to the wire (off to the sides, at right angles to it), and almost nothing straight off the ends. That's worth knowing before you orient one: broadside is where the signal goes.

Polarization — introduced back in T3A as a property of the wave — is, for an antenna, described by the orientation of the electric field it radiates, which in practice just means the physical orientation of the antenna element itself: a dipole mounted horizontally radiates a horizontally polarized wave; mounted vertically, it radiates vertically. As T3A covered, matching polarization between two stations matters for signal strength on VHF/UHF line-of-sight paths.

Beams and gain

A plain dipole radiates roughly equally in most horizontal directions. A beam antenna (also called a directional antenna) trades that all-around coverage for concentration: it uses multiple elements to focus radiated signal more strongly in one direction, at the cost of radiating less in others — the Yagi is the classic amateur example, and among the antenna types the pool asks about, it's the one that offers the greatest gain.

Antenna gain is exactly that trade quantified: the increase in signal strength in one particular direction, compared to a reference antenna (commonly a theoretical, perfectly uniform "isotropic" radiator). Gain isn't free extra power from nowhere — a beam antenna doesn't radiate more total power than it's fed, it just redistributes the same power to favor one direction over the others, the same way a flashlight reflector doesn't add light, it just concentrates the bulb's existing output into a narrower beam.

Worked example You switch from an isotropic reference antenna to a Yagi aimed at a distant station, feeding both with the identical transmitter power. The distant station reports your signal is noticeably stronger. Did your transmitter suddenly put out more power? No — the Yagi's gain means it's concentrating the same total power more tightly toward that one direction, at the expense of the directions you're not aiming at.

A related trick for shortening an antenna without changing what it resonates at: antenna loading means electrically lengthening an antenna by inserting inductors (coils) into the radiating element — useful when a physically full-size antenna (say, a full quarter-wave HF whip) won't fit on a vehicle or handheld. Loading trades size for some efficiency, which is directly why a handheld radio's short, flexible "rubber duck" antenna has lower efficiency than a full-size quarter-wave antenna on the same band — it's electrically shortened, and that shortening costs you signal.

A few more antenna-length and orientation facts the pool tests directly:

  • Physically shortening a dipole raises its resonant frequency, and lengthening it lowers the resonant frequency — there's simply less wire for the signal to travel across. That's the opposite effect from loading: inserting coils (or adding capacitive loading at the tips) electrically lengthens the antenna without changing its physical size, which lowers its resonant frequency instead — the same lowering effect, whether the loading is inductive or capacitive.
  • A 19-inch vertical antenna is a common sight on 2 meters because that length works out to a resonant quarter-wave at 2-meter frequencies (146 MHz), following the same wavelength math from T3B.
  • A 5/8-wavelength whip (longer than a plain quarter-wave) is popular for VHF/UHF mobile use specifically because it has more gain than a 1/4-wave antenna — it concentrates more of its radiation toward the horizon, where distant stations actually are, instead of wasting some of it straight up.
  • Operating a handheld transceiver inside a vehicle without an external antenna loses signal strength mainly because the vehicle's metal body shields the antenna — the car acts like a partial cage around it.

T9B

Feed lines, SWR & antenna tuners

A feed line is simply the cable that carries RF energy between your radio and your antenna. The overwhelming majority of amateur stations use coaxial cable ("coax") — a center conductor surrounded by insulation, then a braided or foil shield, then an outer jacket — mainly because it's easy to route, doesn't need to be kept clear of nearby metal the way some other feed lines do, and requires few special installation considerations. Amateur coax is almost always 50 ohms characteristic impedance, chosen to match the output impedance that most transmitters and antennas are designed around.

No feed line is perfectly lossless — some of the RF energy travelling down it is always absorbed as heat rather than delivered to the antenna. Coax loss gets worse as frequency increases: the same cable that's quite efficient feeding an HF antenna loses noticeably more of your power feeding a UHF one. Loss also comes from less obvious sources: water getting into connectors, a high SWR on the line, and simply having multiple connectors in the run (each one a small extra loss point) are all real, independent contributors — which is also why outdoor coax connectors like PL-259, BNC, and Type N should all be carefully weatherproofed with tape, regardless of which of those three you're using.

Not all coax is equal. Between RG-58 (thin, flexible) and RG-213 (thicker), the electrical difference that matters is that RG-213 has less loss at a given frequency — the extra bulk is mostly dielectric and shielding that reduces how much signal is absorbed per foot. Carried further, air-insulated hardline — coax that uses air rather than solid plastic as its insulator — has the lowest loss of the common feed line types, which is why it shows up on long HF/VHF runs where every dB matters, despite being stiffer and more awkward to route than flexible coax.

Connectors

Three connector types come up repeatedly. The PL-259 ("UHF connector," confusingly) is a threaded, screw-on connector that's inexpensive and easy to solder, and is the common choice at HF and VHF frequencies — but it isn't weatherproof on its own and isn't the best performer at very high frequencies. The Type N connector is the better choice above 400 MHz, where a PL-259's mechanical design starts introducing more loss and impedance irregularity than a Type N's more precise, weather-resistant design. BNC connectors are, by contrast, a small, quick-disconnect bayonet-type connector common on handhelds and test gear — twist-and-lock instead of threaded.

Worked example You're building a feed line run to a 70-cm (440 MHz) antenna and deciding between PL-259 and Type N connectors. Which is the better choice? 440 MHz is above the 400 MHz point where PL-259 connectors start performing worse — so Type N is the better choice here, even though PL-259 would have been perfectly fine on HF or 2 meters.

SWR: is the load matched?

SWR (standing wave ratio) measures how well a load — typically the antenna system — is matched to the transmission line feeding it. As T4 covered from the meter side, a perfect match reads 1:1 and means all the power reaching the antenna radiates outward; the worse the match, the more power reflects back down the feed line instead. SWR that suddenly starts changing erratically, with no other change to the station, is a strong sign of a loose connection somewhere in the antenna or feed line — a mechanical fault, not a propagation effect.

When your antenna's impedance doesn't naturally match your transceiver's output impedance (and therefore SWR is higher than you'd like), an antenna tuner (also called an antenna coupler) is the fix: its major function is matching the antenna system's impedance to the transceiver's output impedance, using adjustable inductors and capacitors to cancel out the mismatch. A tuner doesn't change what the antenna itself is doing out in space, and it doesn't reduce loss that's already happening in the feed line — it just presents the transmitter with the load impedance it wants to see, so the transmitter itself operates efficiently and safely.

Worked example Your transceiver reports a high SWR on a particular band, but a check confirms every connector is tight and dry. You add an antenna tuner between the radio and the feed line, and the transceiver now reports a clean 1:1 match. Did the tuner fix the mismatch between your antenna and free space? No — the tuner matched the impedance your transmitter sees at its output to what it expects, which lets the transmitter run efficiently; whatever mismatch exists further out at the antenna itself, and any loss that mismatch causes in the feed line before the tuner, is unchanged.

One RF-safety note that pairs directly with T0's exposure material: never work on or reach near a transmitting antenna, and keep people clear of an antenna's near field while transmitting, since RF energy right at the element can be far stronger than the same signal is once it has spread out at a distance.

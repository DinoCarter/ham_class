T7A

Station equipment: the basic building blocks

A receiver pulls a signal out of the air; a transmitter puts one there; a transceiver is simply a device that combines both in one box, sharing circuitry like the VFO and the display between receive and transmit. Two words describe how good a receiver is at its job, and they measure genuinely different things:

  • Sensitivity is a receiver's ability to detect the presence of a signal at all — how weak a signal it can pull out of the noise floor.
  • Selectivity is a receiver's ability to discriminate between multiple signals — how well it rejects a strong station sitting right next to the one you're trying to copy.
Worked example Two receivers, side by side. Receiver A pulls a barely-there signal out of the noise that Receiver B can't hear at all — but when two stations are close together in frequency, Receiver A turns into mush while Receiver B cleanly separates them. Receiver A has better sensitivity; Receiver B has better selectivity. A radio can be strong in one and mediocre in the other — they're independent specs, not two names for the same thing.

Inside either half of a transceiver, a handful of named stages do the actual work:

Receive chain

Antenna → optional RF preamplifier (boosts a weak incoming signal before anything else touches it) → mixer → demodulator → audio.

Transmit chain

Microphone/data → oscillator + mixer create the RF carrier → modulation combines your speech with that carrier → optional external RF power amplifier boosts the output → antenna.

An oscillator is the circuit that generates a signal at one specific frequency — it's the reference tone everything else gets built from or compared against. The Variable Frequency Oscillator (VFO) is the particular oscillator whose job is setting your actual receive and transmit frequency (the same VFO knob from T4B). A mixer is what converts a signal from one frequency to another: feed it two signals and it outputs their sum and difference frequencies, which is exactly the trick a receiver uses to slide an incoming signal down to a frequency its filters and demodulator are built for.

A transverter does something related but different: it converts the RF input and output of an existing transceiver to another band entirely, rather than converting within one receiver's internal stages.

Worked example You own a 2-meter all-mode transceiver, and want to get on 10 GHz — a band your radio has no idea exists. Add a transverter built for that band: on transmit, it takes your transceiver's 2-meter output and shifts it up to 10 GHz; on receive, it shifts incoming 10 GHz signals back down to 2 meters for your transceiver to handle normally. The transceiver still does all the real work — generating and demodulating SSB, CW, whatever mode you picked — the transverter's only job is the frequency shift in both directions.

A few more concrete, function-level facts round out this group: an RF power amplifier is what you add after a transceiver's output to increase transmitted power. The transceiver's PTT (push-to-talk) input switches it from receive to transmit when grounded — that's the actual electrical mechanism behind pressing the mic button. And on some VHF power amplifiers with a separate SSB/CW-FM mode switch, that switch's job is simply to set the amplifier for proper operation in whichever mode you selected — SSB needs the amplifier's stages biased to stay linear (preserve the exact shape of a voice waveform), while CW and FM don't, so the amplifier's internal setup differs between them even though the frequency range doesn't change.

T7B

Symptoms, causes & cures of common problems

Four words get thrown around loosely in interference discussions, and the exam wants you to keep them straight, because the fix is different for each:

  • Overload (or "fundamental overload") happens at someone else's receiver: your strong, nearby, perfectly legal signal simply overwhelms a nearby radio's front end, which can't reject a strong out-of-band signal — so it "hears" you even though you're nowhere near its frequency.
  • Overdrive happens at your own transmitter: feeding a stage more audio or drive than it can handle cleanly — too much microphone gain is the classic case — makes it distort and splatter outside the bandwidth it should occupy.
  • Intermodulation ("intermod") is what happens when two or more signals mix together inside a nonlinear stage (an overloaded front end or amplifier) and produce brand-new phantom frequencies that nobody actually transmitted.
  • A spurious emission is any unwanted transmitter output outside your intended signal; a harmonic — an exact integer multiple of your operating frequency — is the most common specific kind.

All four of those — fundamental overload, harmonics, and other spurious emissions — can cause radio-frequency interference. Which one you're dealing with changes where you aim the fix:

Worked example Your 2-meter (146 MHz) transmission is bleeding into a neighbor's FM broadcast radio at 98.5 MHz. Is that a harmonic? Check the math: a harmonic has to be an exact whole multiple of 146 MHz (146, 292, 438…), and 98.5 isn't one of those. So this is much more likely plain fundamental overload of the broadcast radio's front end by your nearby strong signal — and the fix follows from that diagnosis: install a filter at the broadcast radio's own antenna input, not changes to your transmitter, since your signal isn't actually doing anything wrong.

That "fix it at the affected receiver" principle generalizes: to reduce interference your station is causing to a non-amateur receiver, the filter belongs on the affected receiver's antenna input. To reduce interference a nearby commercial FM station is causing to your own 2-meter receiver, a band-reject (notch) filter tuned to that FM station's frequency, installed at your receiver, keeps its strong signal from overloading your front end.

When a neighbor reports interference, the accepted troubleshooting order is: first, confirm your own station is functioning properly and isn't even interfering with your own radio or TV when it's tuned to the same channel — before assuming the problem is on their end. If the problem turns out to be something in the neighbor's home causing interference to your station instead, the constructive path is the same in reverse: work with them to identify the offending device, politely explain that FCC rules do restrict interference-causing devices, and make sure your own station still meets good amateur practice throughout. For non-fiber cable-TV interference specifically, the first troubleshooting step is mundane: make sure every coax connector in the TV feed line is installed properly — a loose or poorly-shielded fitting is often the entire problem, before you reach for a filter at all.

A separate, very ordinary source of distorted repeater audio reports is nothing exotic: being slightly off frequency, talking too loudly or too close to the microphone, or simply operating from a bad location can each cause it on their own. Talking too loudly or too close is specifically what a report of over-deviating means — your voice is pushing the FM carrier's frequency swing wider than the receiver's filters are built to pass cleanly — and the direct fix is simply to back away from the microphone or speak less loudly, which reduces how far you're driving that swing. One less obvious fix: a clip-on ferrite choke around the microphone cable can eliminate distorted transmissions caused by your own transmitted RF sneaking back into the audio path through that cable (the cable acts as an unintentional antenna) — the choke adds impedance at radio frequencies without touching the low-frequency audio signal riding on the same wire.

T7C

Antenna & transmission line troubleshooting

T4A already introduced the SWR meter as a station accessory; T7C is about what a bad reading actually does to your station, and how to measure and diagnose it. Two dedicated instruments come up: a dummy load is a 50-ohm non-inductive resistor mounted on a heat sink that you connect in place of an antenna specifically so test transmissions never actually go out over the air — the resistor absorbs the RF power and turns it into heat instead of radiating it. An antenna analyzer is what you use to determine whether an antenna is actually resonant at your desired operating frequency, without needing a full transmitter and SWR meter setup.

SWR itself reads as a ratio: 1:1 is a perfect impedance match between antenna and feed line (nothing reflected); 4:1 indicates a real impedance mismatch, with a meaningful fraction of your power bouncing back down the line instead of radiating. A directional wattmeter is the instrument that actually determines SWR — it separately measures forward power and reflected power, and computes the ratio from those two numbers.

Worked example You measure 4:1 SWR on your antenna and notice your solid-state radio's output power has quietly dropped, too. Coincidence? No — most solid-state transmitters deliberately reduce output power as SWR climbs past a certain point specifically to protect the RF output transistors from the voltage stresses that reflected power causes. And where does the power that doesn't radiate actually go? Power lost in a feed line is converted into heat — the same physics as the dummy load's resistor, just spread out along the length of the cable instead of concentrated in one component.

Coaxial cable itself fails for mundane physical reasons more often than electrical ones. Moisture contamination is a leading cause of coax failure — water getting into the cable degrades it over time. That's exactly why a cable's outer jacket needs to be UV-resistant: sunlight can damage the jacket and let water enter the cable, which is the real long-term threat, not the UV itself interacting with the RF. Between dielectric types, foam-dielectric coax has an advantage over solid-dielectric: less loss per foot, which is why it's the common choice for long feed line runs.

T7D

Basic test instruments & soldering

Three meters, three different jobs, and each has to be connected differently because of what it's actually measuring:

  • A voltmeter measures electric potential (voltage) and connects in parallel with a component — it just "taps in" across two points to measure the difference between them, without needing to interrupt the circuit's current path.
  • An ammeter measures electric current, and connects in series — it has to physically become part of the path, since the current you're measuring has to flow through the meter itself.
  • An ohmmeter measures resistance by applying a small current from its own internal battery and measuring the resulting voltage — it's doing Ohm's Law in reverse, solving for resistance instead of assuming it.

A multimeter combines all three functions (plus often more) in one instrument with a selector switch, and is what most hams actually own. That internal battery in the resistance function is exactly why two things matter: never attempt to measure voltage while a multimeter is set to its resistance (ohms) function — the circuit's own voltage fighting the meter's internal battery is what actually damages multimeters — and always make sure a circuit is unpowered before measuring in-circuit resistance, for the same reason.

Worked example You clip an ohmmeter across a large, fully discharged capacitor and watch the reading climb steadily over a few seconds instead of settling immediately. What's happening? The ohmmeter's own small internal current is charging the capacitor as it measures: at first, current flows into the capacitor easily (reads as low resistance), and as the capacitor charges up, less current flows for the same applied voltage, so the meter reads a higher and higher resistance over time. That climbing reading is actually a normal, diagnostic sign you're looking at a large capacitor, not a fault.

Soldering has its own short list of hard rules. Use rosin-core solder for electronics — never acid-core solder, which is meant for plumbing and leaves a corrosive residue that eats away at fine wires and circuit board traces over time. A good joint looks bright and shiny; a cold solder joint (one that never got hot enough to properly flow and bond) looks rough or lumpy instead, and can turn into an unreliable, intermittent, or high-resistance connection later even if it looks fine at a glance today.

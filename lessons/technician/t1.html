Commission's Rules

Every right-of-way privilege you get on the air — which frequencies, which modes, how much power — comes from one document: FCC Part 97 (Title 47 of the Code of Federal Regulations, Part 97). T1 is the FCC-rules subelement: who's in charge, what you're licensed to do, and what you're explicitly not. A citation like §97.1 below means "Part 97, Section 1" — you can look up the full text of any of them at law.cornell.edu/cfr.

T1A

Purpose, authority & basic terms

The rules start by saying why the Amateur Radio Service exists at all. Under §97.1, its basis and purpose is:

  1. Recognizing ham radio's value to the public as a voluntary, noncommercial service — particularly for emergency communications
  2. Continuing amateurs' proven ability to advance the radio art
  3. Encouraging skill in both the technical and communication phases of the art
  4. Expanding the pool of trained operators, technicians, and electronics experts
  5. Continuing amateurs' unique ability to build international goodwill

None of that is enforced informally — the FCC regulates and enforces Part 97 in the United States, full stop; the ARRL is an advocacy and membership organization, not a regulator. One consequence of "voluntary noncommercial service" that trips people up immediately: causing willful or malicious interference to other stations is flatly prohibited (§97.101(d)), regardless of who's "right" about a frequency.

A few terms get defined precisely here because later rules depend on them:

  • A space station is an amateur station located more than 50 km above Earth's surface (§97.3(a)(41)) — that's the definition the satellite-operation questions in T8 build on.
  • A beacon is an automatically controlled station transmitting one-way for propagation observation. On HF, beacons are confined to specific slices of the Technician bands — on 10 meters, that's 28.200–28.300 MHz (§97.203(d)).
  • A Frequency Coordinator is a volunteer — not an FCC office — selected by amateurs in a local or regional area who recommends repeater and auxiliary station channels, to keep nearby repeaters from stepping on each other (§97.3(a)(22)).
  • RACES (Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service) stations need something beyond the operator license: certification of current enrollment by a civil defense organization (§97.407(a)).

Two more everyday mechanics: phonetic alphabets ("Alpha Bravo Charlie…") are encouraged, not required, when using phone emissions (§97.119(b)(2)) — we'll come back to why in T1F. And since the FCC stopped mailing paper licenses, your proof of license isn't a certificate or an email — it's that your grant appears in the FCC's ULS database (§97.7), with notification arriving by email with a link to your grant (§97.23).

Worked example You pass your exam and get a Certificate of Successful Completion of Examination (CSCE) on the spot. Are you licensed yet? Not quite — the CSCE proves you passed, but your actual operating authority doesn't exist until the grant is processed and shows up in the FCC's license database (ULS), usually within a few business days. This exact mechanic comes back in T1C.

T1B

Frequency privileges

Part 97 grants specific frequency ranges to each license class in §97.301, and specifies what modes are allowed where in §97.305. Some of those bands are secondary allocations — the amateur service shares them with another radio service that has priority. In a secondary segment, hams must avoid interfering with the primary users and must accept interference from them; there's no "first come, first served" (§97.303).

Technician gets essentially the full amateur privileges at 50 MHz and above, plus one narrow slice of HF:

HF

10 meters: 28.000–28.500 MHz only (§97.301(e)). No privileges on 80/40/20/15 meters.

VHF / UHF / SHF

Same bands as General/Extra, from 6 meters up (table below).

Four mode words come up constantly from here on, so it's worth pinning them down before looking at a band chart that uses them as labels:

  • CW (Continuous Wave) is Morse code: the transmitter's carrier is switched fully on and off in short and long pulses that spell out letters, and nothing else about the signal varies. It's called "continuous" because the carrier itself is a steady, unbroken sine wave while it's on — unlike antique spark-gap transmitters, which produced decaying pulses instead of a clean wave. Because it packs information into such a narrow sliver of spectrum, CW gets a special privilege: it's legal on any frequency authorized to the control operator, even inside segments that otherwise restrict you to other modes.
  • Phone just means voice — talking, like on a telephone. SSB, AM, and FM are all "phone" emissions; Part 97 lumps them together when it allocates a phone segment of a band, and leaves which specific voice mode you use up to you.
  • Image means pictures instead of voice or text — slow-scan television (SSTV) and facsimile are the amateur examples. Many HF segments are shared between phone and image, which is why you'll see them written together as "phone/image."
  • Data covers digital modes that send text or bits directly rather than sound — RTTY (radioteletype) and newer digital modes like FT8 are examples.

The 10-meter slice, up close

That special CW privilege from §97.305(a) is why the explorer shows CW as legal across the whole 28.000–28.500 MHz slice, not just the data/RTTY portion — it rides on top of whatever the primary mode restriction of a segment is. Power in Technician's HF segments is capped at 200 W PEP (§97.313) — lower than the 1500 W PEP ceiling that applies above 30 MHz (§97.313(b), with some specific segment exceptions).

Worked example You want to make an SSB voice contact at 28.400 MHz running 250 W PEP. Legal? Check the mode first: 28.400 MHz falls in the 28.300–28.500 MHz phone/image segment, so SSB voice is allowed there. Now check power: Technician's HF segments are capped at 200 W PEP, and 250 W exceeds that — so as-described, it's not legal. Turn the power down to 200 W or less and you're clear.

A couple of related mechanics worth knowing: SSB phone is available in at least some segment of every one of these bands above 50 MHz (§97.305(c)); digital modes like FT8 are authorized on 10, 6, and 2 meters for Technician; and any Technician-or-higher licensee can contact the International Space Station on VHF (§97.301, §97.207(c)) — no special NASA approval needed. Finally, never set your transmit frequency right at a band edge: modulation sidebands can spill past the edge, your display might have calibration error, and your transmitter can drift — any one of those can put you outside the band without you noticing (§97.101(a), §97.301(a-e)).

T1C

Licensing

The FCC currently issues new licenses for three classes: Technician, General, and Amateur Extra (§97.9(a), §97.17(a)). Novice and Advanced still exist as grandfathered classes for people who already hold them, but you can't newly earn one.

Call signs come in two flavors. A sequential call sign is assigned automatically from the next available slot for your license class and region. A vanity call sign is one you request specifically — and unlike some other countries' systems, any licensed amateur may apply for a vanity call sign (§97.19), not just upper-class licensees.

A license, once granted, runs on a fixed clock:

Granted
(10‑yr term starts)
Renewal window opens
(90 days out)
Expires → 2‑yr grace period
(no operating privileges)

The normal license term is ten years (§97.25). You can request renewal up to 90 days before expiration (§97.21, §1.949). If it lapses anyway, there's a two-year grace period to renew without retaking the exam — but you may not transmit during the grace period until the renewal is actually granted (§97.21(a)(b)). And the FCC needs to be able to reach you: if it can't (e.g., a dead email on file), your operator license can be suspended or your station license revoked (§97.23).

One more timing question that trips people up: once you pass the exam, when can you legally transmit? Not immediately on receiving your CSCE, and not when something arrives in the mail — only once your grant actually appears in the FCC's license database (§97.5a). Same mechanic as T1A's proof-of-license point, just from the other direction.

A narrower note on scope: FCC-licensed amateurs may operate from a US-documented vessel in international waters with the master's permission (§97.5(a)(2), §97.11(a)), and international communications are limited to matters "incidental to the purposes of the Amateur Radio Service and remarks of a personal character" (§97.117) — not business, not broadcasting. That restriction is the seed of all of T1D.

T1D

Authorized & prohibited transmissions

Go back to T1A's basis and purpose: noncommercial, personal-character, technical/emergency/ goodwill. Everything in T1D is that principle turning into hard bans — with a handful of narrow, specific carve-outs:

  • Broadcasting — one-way transmissions meant for the general public — is banned outright (§97.3(a)(10) defines it, §97.113(b)/§97.111(b) ban it).
  • Communications with a country whose government has told the ITU it objects are prohibited (§97.111(a)(1)) — not an ARRL or IARU list, an ITU one.
  • Indecent or obscene language is prohibited, no exceptions (§97.113(a)(4)).
  • Getting paid to operate a station is generally prohibited — except specific carve-outs, like when the communication is part of classroom instruction at an educational institution (§97.113(a)(3)(iii)).
  • You can mention your own radio gear is for sale — but not as a regular business, and only for equipment that's actually your own property (§97.113(a)(3)(ii)).
  • Music is banned except when incidental to an authorized retransmission of manned spacecraft communications (§97.113(a)(4)/(c)) — a narrow exception, not a general one.
  • Messages encoded to hide their meaning are banned except for control commands sent to space stations or model craft (§97.211(b), §97.215(b), §97.113(a)(4)).
  • Supporting broadcasting, program production, or news gathering is allowed only when directly related to immediate safety of life or protection of property, and no other means is available (§97.113(5)(b)).

An auxiliary station is a specific, allowed kind of one-way link — for example, a link between a remote repeater receiver and the main repeater transmitter (§97.113(d), §97.201(e)). And on-the-air test transmissions are fine, but you still have to identify the transmitting station while you do it (§97.119(a)).

Worked example Can you play a song over a local repeater to test your audio levels? No — music is banned except for the narrow manned-spacecraft-retransmission case, which this isn't. Use your own voice or a standard test pattern instead, and remember to identify while you do it.

T1E

Control operator

A station may never transmit without a control operator (§97.7(a)) — even a fully automatic repeater has one, it's just not sitting at the microphone. Four definitions carry the whole group:

  • A control operator is the amateur designated by the station licensee to be responsible for that station's transmissions and rule compliance (§97.3(a)(13)). The licensee does the designating (§97.103(b)).
  • The control point is wherever the control operator function is actually performed — not the antenna, not necessarily the transmitter (§97.3(a)(14)).
  • Automatic control means no one is actively at the control point — a repeater is the textbook example (§97.3(a)(6), §97.205(d)).
  • Remote control means the control operator isn't at the station but operates it via a control link — operating over the internet is the textbook example (§97.3(a)(39)). Any station may be remotely controlled, not just certain types (§97.109(c)).

Here's the part with real consequences: the control operator's license class determines the station's transmitting privileges at that moment — not the station licensee's class, not the radio's capabilities, not whoever else happens to be in the room (§97.103(b)). And when the control operator isn't the licensee, both of them share responsibility for proper operation (§97.103(a)).

Worked example A club's station is licensed with an Amateur Extra Class trustee, but tonight a Technician-class member is at the radio, designated as control operator for the session. What can they do? Technician privileges only — the trustee's license class is irrelevant to what's legal right now, because the rules key off whoever the control operator actually is. (Consistent with this: outside of an emergency, a Technician can never be the control operator of a station transmitting in an Amateur-Extra-only band segment, no matter who else is on the license — §97.301.)

T1F

Station identification, repeaters & third parties

The identification rule is one number: identify with your FCC-assigned call sign at least every 10 minutes during a contact, and at the end (§97.119(a)). That applies even if you're using a tactical call like "Race Headquarters" on the air — the tactical call doesn't replace your FCC call sign, it just supplements it.

Phone identification must be in English (§97.119(b)(2)) — and you may send your call sign using CW or phone even on a phone transmission (§97.119(b)(2)). This is also where the phonetic alphabet from T1A pays off: it's encouraged specifically because clear phone identification matters. Self-assigned portable/mobile indicators are flexible — "stroke," "slant," and "slash" (as in "KL7CC stroke W3") are all acceptable ways to say the same thing (§97.119(c)).

A repeater simultaneously retransmits another station's signal on a different channel (§97.3(a)(40)). If a repeater inadvertently retransmits something that violates the rules, accountability falls on the originating station's control operator, not the repeater's (§97.205(g)) — repeaters aren't liable for what passes through them, but that also means the burden is on every user to self-police.

Third-party communications are messages a control operator sends to another station's control operator on behalf of someone else (§97.3(a)(47)). With a foreign station, that's only allowed if the two countries have a third-party agreement (§97.115(a)(2)) — and if an unlicensed person speaks over the air to that foreign station, the licensed control operator still has to do the actual station identification.

Two closing mechanics: the station licensee must make the station and its records available any time an FCC representative asks — no advance written notice required (§97.103(c)). And a club station license needs the club to have at least four members (§97.5(b)(2)).

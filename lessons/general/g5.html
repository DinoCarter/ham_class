Electrical Principles

G5 is where Technician's T5 vocabulary — reactance, impedance, capacitance, inductance, decibels — turns into actual arithmetic. Nothing here goes past algebra, but the pool expects you to run the numbers, not just recognize the words. Every formula below comes with a worked example, and every worked-example answer here matches an actual question in the pool unless the text says otherwise.

G5A

Reactance, inductance, capacitance, impedance & resonance

Reactance — already defined back in T5C as "the opposition capacitors and inductors present to AC current" — gets its own symbol and unit here: it's written with the letter X, and like plain resistance it's measured in ohms. The two components that produce it behave in opposite ways as frequency changes, and the pool tests that opposite behavior directly:

Inductive reactance (XL)

XL = 2πfL

Rises as frequency rises. An inductor resists a faster-changing current more strongly, so higher frequency means more opposition.

Capacitive reactance (XC)

XC = 1 ÷ (2πfC)

Falls as frequency rises. A capacitor charges and discharges more freely as the current reverses faster, so higher frequency means less opposition.

f = frequency (Hz), L = inductance (henrys), C = capacitance (farads). Both formulas share the constant 2πf, which is often written as ω (angular frequency) in more advanced texts — you won't need that shorthand for the exam, just the two formulas above.

Worked example (illustrative — formula application, not a pool question) A 10 µH inductor at 3.5 MHz: XL = 2π × 3,500,000 × 0.00001 = about 220 ohms. The same inductor at twice the frequency (7 MHz) would show twice the reactance, about 440 ohms — reactance and frequency move together for an inductor.
Worked example (illustrative — formula application, not a pool question) A 100 pF capacitor at 14 MHz: XC = 1 ÷ (2π × 14,000,000 × 0.0000000001) = about 114 ohms. Double the frequency to 28 MHz and XC is cut in half, about 57 ohms — reactance and frequency move opposite ways for a capacitor.

Impedance and admittance

Impedance (symbol Z) is resistance and reactance combined, and its formal definition on the exam is simply the ratio of voltage to current: Z = E ÷ I, the AC/RF equivalent of Ohm's Law, measured in ohms. Its inverse has its own name and symbol: admittance (Y = 1 ÷ Z) is exactly what the name suggests — how easily a circuit admits current, rather than how much it opposes it — measured in siemens.

Impedance matching at radio frequencies isn't limited to one device or trick — the pool credits a transformer, a Pi-network, and a length of transmission line all equally as valid ways to match one impedance to another, since they all accomplish the same underlying goal (maximum power transfer between two circuits that don't natively share the same impedance) through different physical mechanisms.

Resonance: when reactances cancel

Wire an inductor and a capacitor together and their reactances work against each other in a specific way: inductive reactance and capacitive reactance are, mathematically, opposite in sign, so in a series LC circuit they partially cancel. At one particular frequency — the resonant frequency — they cancel completely (XL = XC), which is the textbook definition of resonance for this pool. In a series LC circuit at resonance, with the two reactances gone, all that's left opposing current is whatever small resistance the circuit has — so impedance drops to a very low value right at resonance, the opposite of what you might guess. The frequency where this happens follows its own formula, built from the same L and C that determine XL and XC:

Resonant frequency

f = 1 ÷ (2π√(LC))

f = resonant frequency (Hz), L = inductance (henrys), C = capacitance (farads)

Worked example (illustrative — formula application, not a pool question) A 10 µH inductor and a 100 pF capacitor in series: LC = 0.00001 × 0.0000000001 = 1 × 10−15. The square root of that is about 3.16 × 10−8, and f = 1 ÷ (2π × 3.16 × 10−8) ≈ 5.0 MHz. Check it against the other two formulas: at 5.0 MHz that same 10 µH inductor has XL ≈ 314 ohms, and the 100 pF capacitor has XC ≈ 318 ohms — close enough (the small gap is rounding) to confirm they're cancelling right around 5.0 MHz.

G5B

The decibel, current/voltage dividers, power calculations & RMS

T5B's decibel facts still hold at General level: a doubling of power is still +3 dB. General adds the ability to work with dB as a percentage loss or gain, not just a multiplier. A loss of 1 dB corresponds to a power ratio of 10−0.1, which works out to about 79.4% of the original power remaining — in other words, a 20.6% power loss. That's a genuinely useful number: a 1 dB loss sounds tiny, but it's actually about a fifth of your power gone, which is exactly why keeping feed-line and connector losses well under 1 dB matters on a real HF station.

Worked example A lossy feed line and connector combination costs you 1 dB. What fraction of your transmitter power never reaches the antenna? 10(−1⁄10) = 10−0.1 ≈ 0.794, so about 79.4% of your power arrives and about 20.6% is lost — matching the pool's answer of approximately 20.6 percent.

Current dividers: extending T5D's parallel-circuit fact

T5D already established that every branch of a parallel circuit sees the same voltage. General adds the current side of that same picture: in a circuit of parallel resistors, the total current equals the sum of the currents through each individual branch — current entering the parallel combination splits up among the branches (more through a lower-resistance branch, less through a higher-resistance one), then recombines, so adding up every branch's current always accounts for all of it.

DC power, three ways

T5C already gave the basic DC power formula, P = I × E. Because Ohm's Law lets you substitute for either I or E, that one formula expands into three equivalent forms, and the pool expects you to pick whichever form fits the numbers you're actually given:

Given I and E

P = I × E

Given E and R

P = E² ÷ R

Given I and R

P = I² × R

Worked example — given voltage and resistance 400 VDC applied to an 800-ohm load. P = E² ÷ R = 400² ÷ 800 = 160,000 ÷ 800 = 200 watts.
Worked example — given voltage and current A 12 VDC light bulb draws 0.2 amperes. P = I × E = 0.2 × 12 = 2.4 watts.
Worked example — given current and resistance 7.0 milliamperes (0.007 A) flows through a 1,250-ohm resistor. P = I² × R = 0.007² × 1,250 = 0.000049 × 1,250 = 0.06125 watts, or about 61 milliwatts.

RMS: the value that makes AC power math work like DC power math

Every power formula above assumes a steady DC voltage or current. AC voltage is constantly changing — sweeping from zero up to a peak, back through zero, down to a negative peak, and back — so "the" voltage of an AC signal isn't a single number unless you define one deliberately. RMS (root-mean-square) voltage is that definition: it's the specific AC value that produces the same power dissipation in a resistor as a DC voltage of the same number would. That's the whole point of RMS — it lets you plug an AC circuit's voltage straight into the same P = E²÷R formula you'd use for DC, without any extra correction, as long as the E you use is the RMS value.

For a plain sine wave specifically (the shape of household AC and an unmodulated RF carrier), RMS relates to the peak value by one fixed ratio:

Peak → RMS

RMS = peak × 0.707

RMS → peak

peak = RMS × 1.414

0.707 and 1.414 are reciprocals of each other (1 ÷ 1.414 = 0.707) and both trace back to √2 ≈ 1.41421 — a property of the sine shape itself, not something you need to derive on the exam, just recognize and apply. Peak-to-peak is simply double the peak value, since the wave swings equally positive and negative.

Worked example — RMS to peak-to-peak A sine wave has an RMS voltage of 120 volts (ordinary household AC). Peak = 120 × 1.414 = 169.7 V. Peak-to-peak is double that: 169.7 × 2 = 339.4 volts.
Worked example — peak to RMS A sine wave peaks at 17 volts. RMS = 17 × 0.707 = about 12 volts.

PEP: applying RMS to a transmitter

PEP (peak envelope power) is the power at the very highest point of a signal's envelope. For an unmodulated carrier (a steady RF tone, with no modulation swinging its amplitude around), the envelope never changes, so its peak and its average are the same value — the PEP-to-average-power ratio for an unmodulated carrier is exactly 1.00. That means an unmodulated carrier with 1,060 watts of average power also has a PEP of 1,060 watts, full stop, no conversion needed.

Computing PEP from a measured RF voltage combines RMS with the power formula directly. Given a peak-to-peak voltage across a known load (commonly a 50-ohm dummy load on a test bench), the steps are: halve peak-to-peak to get peak, multiply by 0.707 to get RMS, then square and divide by resistance.

Worked example 200 volts peak-to-peak measured across a 50-ohm dummy load. Peak = 200 ÷ 2 = 100 V. RMS = 100 × 0.707 = 70.7 V. PEP = RMS² ÷ R = 70.7² ÷ 50 = 5,000 ÷ 50 = 100 watts.
Worked example 500 volts peak-to-peak across the same 50-ohm load. Peak = 250 V. RMS = 250 × 0.707 = 176.8 V. PEP = 176.8² ÷ 50 = 31,255 ÷ 50 ≈ 625 watts.

The same relationship runs in reverse: given power dissipated in a known load, you can solve back for RMS voltage by rearranging P = E²÷R into E = √(P × R).

Worked example — solving backward for RMS voltage A 50-ohm dummy load dissipates 1,200 watts. E = √(P × R) = √(1,200 × 50) = √60,000 ≈ 245 volts RMS.

G5C

Resistors, capacitors & inductors in series and parallel; transformers

T5D already covered resistors in series (add directly) and left parallel resistors as a fact about current, not a formula. Here's the formula General actually tests, and the pattern that ties all three component types together:

Resistors & inductors: same pattern

Series: add directly.

Rtotal = R1 + R2 + …

Parallel: reciprocals add.

1÷Rtotal = 1÷R1 + 1÷R2 + …

Capacitors: the pattern flips

Parallel: add directly.

Ctotal = C1 + C2 + …

Series: reciprocals add.

1÷Ctotal = 1÷C1 + 1÷C2 + …

The one thing worth memorizing deliberately: inductors behave like resistors (series adds directly, parallel takes reciprocals), while capacitors behave the opposite way (parallel adds directly, series takes reciprocals). It's easy to mix these up under exam pressure precisely because capacitors are the odd one out.

Worked example — three resistors in parallel 10, 20, and 50 ohms in parallel: 1÷R = 1÷10 + 1÷20 + 1÷50 = 0.1 + 0.05 + 0.02 = 0.17. R = 1÷0.17 ≈ 5.9 ohms — notably less than the smallest individual resistor, which is always true for resistors in parallel.
Worked example — two resistors in parallel (shortcut) 100 and 200 ohms in parallel. With exactly two resistors, "product over sum" is a shortcut for the same reciprocal formula: (100 × 200) ÷ (100 + 200) = 20,000 ÷ 300 ≈ 67 ohms.
Worked example — capacitors in parallel Two 5.0 nF capacitors and one 750 pF capacitor in parallel. Convert to common units first (750 pF = 0.75 nF): 5.0 + 5.0 + 0.75 = 10.75 nanofarads — parallel capacitors just add.
Worked example — capacitors in series Three 100 µF capacitors in series: 1÷C = 1÷100 + 1÷100 + 1÷100 = 3÷100. C = 100÷3 ≈ 33.3 microfarads. Two 20 µF and 50 µF capacitors in series: 1÷C = 1÷20 + 1÷50 = 0.05 + 0.02 = 0.07. C = 1÷0.07 ≈ 14.3 microfarads.
Worked example — inductors in parallel and in series Three 10 mH inductors in parallel: 1÷L = 3÷10, so L = 10÷3 ≈ 3.3 millihenries — the same reciprocal pattern as parallel resistors. A 20 mH and a 50 mH inductor in series, by contrast, just add: 20 + 50 = 70 millihenries.

One more pair of facts falls straight out of those formulas: to increase capacitance, add a capacitor in parallel (direct addition, same family as the formula above); to increase inductance, add an inductor in series (also direct addition). Both are just the "add directly" half of each component's pattern, applied in the direction that makes the total bigger.

Transformers

A transformer is two coils of wire (a primary and a secondary) wound so their magnetic fields overlap, with no direct electrical connection between them. Applying AC to the primary creates a changing magnetic field, and that field induces a voltage in the secondary winding through mutual inductance — one coil's changing field inducing a voltage in a separate, nearby coil, as opposed to plain inductance, which is a coil inducing a voltage in itself. How much voltage appears on the secondary depends only on the ratio of turns between the two windings:

Transformer turns ratio

Esec ÷ Epri = Nsec ÷ Npri

E = voltage, N = number of turns, on the secondary (sec) and primary (pri) windings

Worked example — step-up transformer A transformer has a 500-turn primary and a 1,500-turn secondary (a 1:3 step-up ratio). Apply 120 VAC to the primary: Esec = Epri × (Nsec÷Npri) = 120 × (1,500÷500) = 120 × 3 = 360 volts.

Run a transformer backward — feed the winding normally used as the secondary, and take output from what's normally the primary — and the turns ratio simply inverts. A transformer built as a 4:1 step-down (voltage divided by 4, primary to secondary) becomes a 4:1 step-up if you feed it in reverse: the output voltage is now the input voltage multiplied by 4.

Because a transformer (ignoring small losses) delivers the same power out as it takes in, P = I × E has to balance on both sides — a winding with higher voltage necessarily carries lower current, and a winding with lower voltage carries higher current. That's exactly why the primary winding of a step-up transformer is wound with heavier wire than the secondary: the primary carries more current (lower voltage, higher current side), and heavier wire is needed to carry that current without excessive resistive heating.

Transformers for impedance matching

Turns ratio doesn't just scale voltage — it scales impedance too, by the square of the turns ratio, which is what makes a transformer one of G5A's valid impedance-matching devices:

Impedance transformation

turns ratio = √(Zpri ÷ Zsec)

Worked example — matching an antenna to coax An antenna's feed point impedance is 600 ohms and it needs to match 50-ohm coaxial cable. Turns ratio = √(600 ÷ 50) = √12 ≈ 3.5 to 1 — a transformer built to that ratio presents 50 ohms on its coax side while presenting 600 ohms on its antenna side, so each side sees the impedance it expects.

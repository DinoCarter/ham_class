Electrical and RF Safety

T0 covered the fundamentals: how RF exposure works, why a fuse belongs in the hot conductor, why tower climbing has hard rules with no exceptions. None of that changes for General — it's still true, and G0 leans on it rather than re-deriving it. What's new is the scale: General-class stations tend to run higher power on more complex antenna systems, which is exactly where RF exposure evaluations, proper wiring, and station grounding stop being optional paperwork and start being the thing standing between you and an actual injury.

G0A

RF safety principles, rules & guidelines; routine station evaluation

T0C already established the core mechanism: RF energy is non-ionizing, and the hazard is heating body tissue (§97.13(c)(1), same mechanism as a microwave oven), not DNA-level damage. G0A is about the practical side: how you actually determine whether your station is within the FCC's MPE limits, and what you do about it if it isn't.

RF exposure from a transmitted signal depends on more than one factor at once — its power density, its frequency, and its duty cycle all matter together, which is why "what determines RF exposure" is correctly answered "all of these," not any one alone. You have three acceptable ways to verify your station complies:

  • Calculation using the method in FCC OET Bulletin 65
  • Calculation by computer modeling
  • Direct measurement of field strength with calibrated equipment

(§97.13(c)(1)) — the rules don't mandate one specific method, and a proper field measurement needs a calibrated field strength meter with a calibrated antenna, not a receiver's signal meter or an SWR meter pressed into service for something it wasn't built to do.

One term worth pinning down precisely: time averaging means your total RF exposure is averaged over a certain period, not measured as an instantaneous peak (§97.13(c)(1)-adjacent guidance) — T0C's duty-cycle math is exactly this in practice. Because averaging is what matters, duty cycle has a direct, testable effect on allowable power: a lower duty cycle permits greater peak power levels for the same time-averaged exposure — the inverse phrasing of T0C's "50% duty cycle doubles the allowable power density" rule, same underlying math either way.

Worked example You calculated your station's MPE compliance for continuous FM at your normal antenna distance and found you're right at the limit. You then switch to CW, which has a lower duty cycle than continuous FM. Does that make your exposure situation better or worse at the same power? Better — a lower duty cycle means less average energy delivered over the averaging period even at identical peak power, which is exactly why a lower duty cycle permits greater power levels to be transmitted for the same MPE compliance. It doesn't exempt you from evaluating at all, though; it just shifts where the line is.

When evaluation shows a problem

If a routine evaluation shows your station's RF energy exceeds permissible limits for possible human exposure, the requirement is direct: take action to prevent human exposure to the excessive RF fields (§97.13(c)(2), §1.1307(b)) — not file paperwork with the FCC, and not get written neighbor permission to operate above the controlled MPE limit. If your station fails the FCC's RF-exposure exemption criteria in the first place (meaning you can't skip evaluation entirely), the required next step is the same: perform an RF exposure evaluation per FCC OET Bulletin 65 (§97.13(c)(2), §1.1307(1)(b)(3)(i)).

Two concrete compliance steps come up together in the pool: perform a routine RF exposure evaluation, and prevent access to any identified high-exposure areas (§97.13(c)(2)) — not posting a copy of the rule text in your shack, and not a blanket neighbor-notification requirement. If evaluation shows a neighbor might get more than the allowable exposure from the main lobe of a directional antenna, the fix is to make sure that lobe simply can't be aimed at them while they're present — not switching to a "non-polarized" antenna (not a meaningful category) and not just picking a higher front-to-back ratio, which doesn't guarantee the main lobe stays clear of them. An indoor transmitting antenna carries the same underlying obligation as any other: make sure MPE limits aren't exceeded in the occupied space around it — proximity to your operating position and physical shielding aren't substitutes for that.

Worked example You run a directional beam on 20 meters, and your evaluation shows that when the beam points directly at your neighbor's yard, they'd receive more than the allowable RF exposure while they're out there. What's the required fix? Not a bigger front-to-back-ratio antenna, and not switching antenna types generally — the specific fix the pool wants is taking precautions that keep the antenna's main lobe from being pointed at them while they're present, whether that's an operating restriction, a rotator interlock, or simply avoiding that heading when you know they're outside.

Finally, scope: which stations are actually subject to these RF-exposure rules? All stations with a time-averaged transmission of more than one milliwatt (§1.1307(1)(b)(3)(i)(A)) — amateur stations are not broadly exempt the way one wrong-answer choice suggests, and the trigger isn't a fixed power number like 500 W or a fixed antenna height; it's that very low one-milliwatt time-averaged threshold, which in practice means essentially every transmitting amateur station is covered.

G0B

Station safety: electrical shock, grounding, fusing, interlocks, wiring & towers

T0A covered the standard 120 V household circuit: black hot, white neutral, green/bare ground, and a fuse or circuit breaker belongs in series with the hot conductor only. A higher-power General station is more likely to touch a 240 V circuit — for a linear amplifier's power supply, say — which has two hot conductors instead of one, plus neutral and ground. The rule generalizes exactly the way you'd expect: in a four-conductor 240 V circuit, only the hot wires get fuses or breakers — never the neutral, never the ground, and every hot wire present needs its own protection, not just one of them.

The other half of T0A's fuse lesson — match the protection to the wire, not the other way around — has real numbers behind it via the National Electrical Code (NEC), which is what actually governs electrical safety of a station (not bandwidth, not modulation limits, not RF exposure — those are separate rules entirely). Two figures the pool tests directly:

Wire → breaker

A 20-ampere circuit breaker requires at least AWG 12 wire, minimum.

Wire → fuse

AWG 14 wire should be protected by no more than a 15-ampere fuse or breaker.

Notice the pattern: thinner wire (14 gauge) pairs with a smaller breaker (15 A); to run a bigger 20 A breaker safely you need thicker wire (12 gauge) underneath it — exactly T0A's space-heater lesson, now with the actual NEC numbers instead of the illustrative 5 A/20 A example.

GFCIs: a different hazard than a fuse

A GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter) solves a problem a normal fuse or breaker can't: it disconnects power specifically when it detects current flowing from a hot wire directly to ground — not hot-to-neutral current (that's just the circuit working normally) and not overvoltage on the hot wires (a fuse doesn't care about voltage, only current). The scenario a GFCI is built for is exactly the one a plain fuse misses: current leaking through a person's body to ground can be far too small to blow a 15 A fuse, yet still be lethal — a GFCI is sensitive to that leak itself, not to the total current draw.

Worked example Your station's AC outlet is protected by a standard 15-amp breaker. Does that alone protect you from being shocked if you touch a faulted hot wire and your body becomes a path to ground? Not necessarily — a fatal shock current through a person can be a small fraction of 15 amps, nowhere near enough to trip an ordinary overcurrent breaker. That's the specific gap a GFCI closes: it trips on the hot-to-ground leak itself, regardless of whether total current ever gets anywhere near the breaker's rating.

Grounding & lightning protection

Building on T0B's bonding rule: a station's lightning protection ground system should be located outside the building (not close to the equipment indoors, not next to a power pole, and not run parallel to a water line) — the whole point is intercepting a surge before it enters the structure. Lightning arrestors on a feed line belong specifically where the feed line enters the building (same principle T0A already established for coax lightning arresters). And every ground point at the station — tower legs, arrestor grounds, everything — must be bonded together with all other grounds (T0B's multiple-grounds-at-different-potentials hazard, restated for General-scale installations).

Interlocks, soldering & generators

A power supply interlock exists for one specific reason: to make sure dangerous voltages are removed if the cabinet is opened — not a thermal shutdown, not warranty protection, not an overvoltage cutoff. It's the mechanical enforcement of T0A's "capacitors can still hold a lethal charge after you switch the supply off" warning: the interlock forces that discharge (or cuts the source) the moment someone actually opens the case to get at it.

A more mundane hazard: lead-tin solder's real danger isn't RF or high voltage at all — it's that lead can contaminate food if you don't wash your hands carefully after handling it. And if you run a backup generator, the operating rule is simple: run it in a well-ventilated area — engine exhaust indoors or in an enclosed space is a carbon-monoxide hazard regardless of how well the generator itself is built or grounded.

Tower work

T0B already established that climbing without a helper or observer is never safe, full stop. General-scale towers add two more habits: before climbing a tower that supports electrically powered devices (rotators, tower-mounted preamps, lighting), make sure all circuits feeding power to the tower are locked out and tagged — not just switched off casually, and definitely not left grounded improperly at the base as a substitute. And when using a safety harness specifically, confirm it's rated for the climber's weight and still within its allowable service life — a harness doesn't stay safe indefinitely just because it looks intact; webbing degrades with age and UV exposure even without visible damage.

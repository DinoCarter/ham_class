Commission's Rules

T1 covered the shape of Part 97: who's in charge, what a control operator is, how identification works. G1 is what General actually buys you inside that same rulebook — real privileges on 80, 40, 20, 15, and the rest of HF, not just the 10-meter sliver Technician gets. The mechanics from T1 (control operators, third-party rules, ITU objections) don't disappear; they just get more consequential once you're running real power on real HF bands, so this lesson builds on T1 rather than re-explaining it. Citations like §97.301 point to the same Part 97 you already know from Technician.

G1A

Control operator frequency privileges; primary & secondary allocations

Technician's HF privilege was one clean slice: 28.000–28.500 MHz, nothing else. General privileges span the rest of HF and MF — 160 through 10 meters, plus 60 meters — but not always the entire band. §97.301(d) draws General's actual table, and the pool's whole point in G1A is teaching you where the holes are.

Four HF bands have a narrow slice carved out exclusively for Amateur Extra licensees: 80, 40, 20, and 15 meters (§97.301(b)) — these are the same four bands where General class licensees "cannot transmit" in part (§97.301(d)). On 40 meters specifically, that Extra-exclusive slice is 7.125 MHz to 7.175 MHz — a General-class control operator may not transmit there. The general pattern repeats on all four bands: Extra gets an exclusive slice near the bottom of the band's CW/data segment and another exclusive slice at the bottom of the phone/image segment; General gets everything else, including the upper frequency portion of the phone/image segment (not the lower) wherever the segment is split. On 15 meters, that means General's phone privilege picks up starting around 21.275 MHz — so 21300 kHz is squarely inside General's 15-meter phone allocation, while, say, transmitting phone at 21.200 MHz would not be.

Full privileges

160, 60†, 30††, 17, 12, and 10 meters — no Extra-exclusive carve-out on these (§97.301(b) lists only 80/40/20/15 as having one).

Shared with Extra

80, 40, 20, 15 meters — General gets everything except a bottom slice of CW/data and a bottom slice of phone/image, both reserved for Extra.

† 60 meters is channelized, not a continuous band — see G1C. †† 30 meters is secondary spectrum shared with non-amateur primary users, CW/data only, no phone — also covered in G1C.

On 10 meters, General is a huge step up from Technician: instead of being capped at 28.000–28.500 MHz, a General-class control operator may use CW across the entire 10-meter band (§97.305(a) — same special CW privilege from T1B, it rides on top of whatever mode restriction otherwise applies) and has full phone privileges across the rest of the band too. The only carve-out on 10 meters is for repeaters: the portion above 29.5 MHz is set aside for repeater use (§97.205(b)) — the rest of the band is direct (simplex/DX) operation.

Worked example You're a fresh General licensee and want to make an SSB phone contact at 7.150 MHz on 40 meters. Legal? No — 7.125–7.175 MHz is the Extra-exclusive slice of 40 meters, and 7.150 MHz falls right inside it. Move up to somewhere in General's phone portion above 7.175 MHz (or below 7.125 MHz in the CW/data segment, using CW) and you're clear.

One more mechanic revisited from T1B/T1D: when the FCC rules designate the amateur service as secondary on a band, amateur stations must not cause harmful interference to the primary users and must accept interference from them (§97.303) — there's no recording call signs, no emergency-only exception, and no time-of-day carve-out. That's the rule 30 meters and parts of 60 meters both run on.

G1B

Antenna structure limitations, good engineering practice & beacon operation

General-class stations tend to mean bigger antennas — taller towers, more metal in the air — which is exactly why G1B exists. Two separate regulatory systems touch your antenna structure, and they're easy to conflate:

  • Part 17 is the FCC's structural rulebook (height marking, lighting, FAA notification) — not Part 97. An antenna structure not near a public-use airport can rise to 200 feet above ground before you need to notify the FAA and register it with the FCC (§97.15(a)). Under 200 feet, away from an airport, none of that paperwork applies.
  • Separately, state and local governments can still regulate amateur antenna structures (zoning, HOA-adjacent ordinances, setback rules) — but not without limit. Under PRB-1, the FCC's 1985 policy on this, local regulation must reasonably accommodate amateur communications and be the minimum practical to accomplish a legitimate local purpose (§97.15(b)) — a town can require a permit or cap a height, but it can't write a rule so restrictive it effectively shuts amateur operation down.
Worked example A local ordinance simply bans all amateur antenna towers over 35 feet, full stop, with no variance process and no consideration of what a given ham actually needs to operate. Is that enforceable as written? Not necessarily — PRB-1 requires local rules to reasonably accommodate amateur communications and be the minimum practical to serve a legitimate purpose. A flat, no-exceptions cap that ignores what's actually needed for reasonable amateur operation is the kind of rule PRB-1 was written to push back on — though it doesn't mean state/local governments have zero say (§97.15(a) still leaves no circumstance where FCC rules take priority "at any time and to any extent," and no circumstance where local rules are barred outright either).

Beacon operation

T1A already defined a beacon as an automatically controlled station transmitting one-way. G1B fills in the operating rules: a beacon's purpose, per the FCC's own definition, is observation of propagation and reception (§97.3(a)(9)) — not repeater identification, not general-interest bulletins, just propagation science. Beacons on HF are confined to 28.20–28.30 MHz (§97.203(d)) — same 10-meter beacon sub-band from T1A. Power is capped at 100 watts PEP output (§97.203(c)), and only one beacon per band from the same station location is allowed (§97.203(b)) — there's no National Beacon Organization coordinating frequencies, and no requirement to publish the frequency anywhere.

A related mechanic worth knowing cold: a specific set of frequencies — 14.100, 18.110, 21.150, 24.930, and 28.200 MHz — hosts a system of propagation beacon stations, which is exactly why amateurs are expected to normally avoid transmitting there (§97.101). It's not a hard legal ban the way a phone/CW segment boundary is — it's operating courtesy toward a shared propagation-monitoring resource.

One-way transmissions, revisited

T1D already established the core ban on one-way broadcasting, with narrow carve-outs. G1B adds two more permitted one-way cases to that list: occasional retransmission of weather and propagation forecasts from US government stations (§97.113(c)), and transmissions that assist someone in learning Morse code (§97.111(5)(b)) — a practice-code broadcast is legal one-way transmission specifically because it's teaching, not programming for the general public. What's still not permitted: regular transmissions offering equipment for sale (even amateur-radio-related equipment) as a standing practice — T1D's "your own gear, occasionally, not as a business" rule still applies.

Two closing mechanics: procedural signals and abbreviations (things like Q signals) are allowed freely, with one condition — they may be used only if they don't obscure the meaning of a message (§97.113(a)(4)), not restricted to an official list. And for everything Part 97 doesn't spell out explicitly, the FCC — not the control operator's judgment, not IEEE, not the ITU — is who determines what counts as "good engineering and good amateur practice" (§97.101(a)).

G1C

Transmitter power, data emission standards & 60-meter operation

The headline number for General: 1500 watts PEP output is the standard power ceiling across most of your new HF privileges — confirmed on the 12-meter band (§97.313), on 28 MHz/10 meters (§97.313), and on 1.8 MHz/160 meters (§97.313). That's the same 1500 W ceiling Amateur Extra runs on those bands — General doesn't get a reduced power limit just for being a lower class, with specific narrow exceptions below.

Two exceptions to memorize, because the exam pool tests them directly against that 1500 W baseline:

  • 30 meters (10.140 MHz): capped at just 200 watts PEP output (§97.313(c)(1)) — a direct consequence of 30 meters being secondary, shared spectrum (G1A's secondary-user rule) with non-amateur primary users who need protecting from a full-power amateur signal.
  • Spread spectrum: capped at 10 watts PEP output (§97.313(j)) — a much steeper cut, since a spread-spectrum signal's whole design point is being hard to detect, and the FCC keeps that combination (stealthy + high-power) off the table.

One measurement detail the pool is precise about: the power limit is always measured as PEP output from the transmitter (§97.313) — not RMS, and not measured at the antenna. Antenna gain doesn't count against your power limit the way it might in some other services; what's regulated is what comes out of the transmitter itself.

Worked example You want to run 1000 W PEP on 10.140 MHz (30 meters) to punch through some interference. Legal? No — 30 meters is capped at 200 W PEP output regardless of band conditions, a much lower ceiling than the 1500 W standard that applies just one band away on 40 or 20 meters. There's no "boost for tough conditions" exception; the 200 W limit exists specifically because 30 meters is secondary, shared spectrum.

New digital protocols

Before using a new, non-standard digital protocol on the air, you must publicly document the technical characteristics of the protocol (§97.309(a)(4)) — no FCC type-certification, no experimental license, no formal rule-making filing required. This traces straight back to T1D's ban on messages encoded to hide their meaning: if nobody outside your own group can find out how your protocol works, it's functionally the same problem as encryption, even if that wasn't the intent.

60-meter operation

60 meters isn't a continuous band the way 40 or 20 meters are — it's a small set of specific channels, and the rules reflect that channelized structure. On USB voice, the maximum occupied bandwidth is 2.8 kHz (§97.303(h)(1)) — tight, compared to a typical wider SSB signal elsewhere on HF, because 60 meters is shared with other (non-amateur) services on a channelized basis and a wider signal risks spilling into an adjacent channel. And there's a specific record-keeping rule that doesn't exist on any other amateur band: if you use an antenna other than a dipole on 60 meters, you must keep a record of that antenna's gain (§97.303(i)) — because 60-meter operation is governed by an effective-radiated-power ceiling relative to a dipole, and the FCC needs a way to confirm a higher-gain antenna isn't pushing your effective power over that ceiling.

G1D

Volunteer Examiners & VECs, temporary identification, element credit

Every amateur exam you've ever taken (or will take for Extra) runs through a two-layer volunteer system: a VEC (Volunteer Examiner Coordinator) is the organization the FCC authorizes to coordinate testing, and individual VEs (Volunteer Examiners), accredited by a VEC, are the licensed hams who actually administer the exam session (§97.509(b)(1)) — the FCC itself isn't in the room.

What a VE is allowed to administer is capped by their own license class: a General class VE may only administer the Technician exam (§97.509(b)(3)(i)) — General privileges don't let you proctor a General or Extra exam; that requires an Amateur Extra VE. Every VE, regardless of class, must be at least 18 years old (§97.509(b)(2)), and a non-US-citizen VE must hold an FCC-granted General class license or higher (§97.509(b)(3)) — there's no separate residency requirement, and no ITU-region restriction on where their home citizenship has to be. Administering a Technician exam session specifically requires at least three VEs of General class or higher present (§97.509(3)(i)(c)).

Worked example You're a newly-minted General licensee and want to help out at a local exam session by serving as one of the VEs. Can you administer the General exam to an upgrading Technician? No — your General class license only qualifies you to administer the Technician (Element 2) exam. Administering General or Extra exams requires an Amateur Extra class VE. You can still be one of the required VEs on a Technician session, though — and you'll need at least two other General-or-higher VEs alongside you, since Technician sessions need three VEs minimum.

Temporary operating authority & the "AG" identifier

T1A and T1C both established that a brand-new license doesn't take effect until the grant shows up in the FCC's ULS database — a CSCE alone doesn't let a first-time applicant transmit. An upgrade works differently. A Technician licensee holding an unexpired CSCE for General privileges may operate on any General or Technician band segment immediately — they don't have to wait for the upgrade to post in the FCC database first (§97.9(b)). The catch: until that upgrade actually shows up in ULS, they must identify by appending "AG" after their call sign whenever operating using General class frequency privileges specifically — not on every transmission, just the ones using privileges their license-of-record doesn't show yet (§97.119(f)(2)).

Worked example You hold KJ7XXX as a Technician and just passed your General exam, CSCE in hand. That evening you want to make an SSB contact at 7.200 MHz (a General-only phone frequency). Legal, and how do you identify? Legal — the CSCE grants you General privileges right away, no ULS wait required. But because your upgrade hasn't posted yet, you must identify as "KJ7XXX stroke AG" (or equivalent) while using that General-only frequency. Back down on a Technician-only segment, plain "KJ7XXX" is fine — the AG suffix only applies when you're actually exercising the new privileges.

A CSCE's element credit is valid for 365 days (§97.9(b)) — long enough to finish paperwork or stack another element, but it does expire.

License terms, expiration & renewal

Building on T1C's ten-year term and two-year grace period: if a previously held license expired and that two-year grace period has also passed, there's no shortcut back in — the applicant must show proof of the expired license's grant and pass the current Element 2 exam again from scratch (§97.505) — not a letter from the FCC, not just showing a copy of the old license. Separately, anyone who once held an FCC-issued General, Advanced, or Amateur Extra class license that wasn't revoked (even if it's now long expired) can receive partial credit for the elements that license represented (§97.501, §97.505(a)) — a specific, narrower rule than "anyone who ever held any US license."

Operating from outside the US

Two remote-control scenarios, and they run on opposite rules: operating a US station by remote control from outside the country still requires the control operator to hold a US operator/primary station license (§97.7) — no special remote-station permit layered on top, just the same license you'd need domestically. But remotely controlling a station physically located in another country (say, a station in South America, operated by internet link from the US) is governed by only that other country's regulations, not the FCC's (§97.507) — you're operating under their rules, in their airspace of spectrum, even though you're sitting at a US keyboard.

G1E

Control categories, repeater regulations, third-party rules, ITU regions & digital stations

North and South America — where every reader of this lesson is almost certainly licensed — fall under ITU Region 2 (§97.301, ITU Radio Regulations). Regions matter because the ITU allocates spectrum slightly differently region by region; the Part 97 band tables you've been studying are specifically the Region 2 allocations.

Cross-band repeaters & control operator class

T1E established that the control operator's license class determines the station's privileges in the moment. G1E adds a repeater-specific wrinkle: a 10-meter repeater may retransmit the 2-meter signal of a station with a Technician class control operator only if the 10-meter repeater's own control operator holds General class or higher (§97.205(b)) — the repeater's privileges (not the originating station's) are what govern whether that 10-meter retransmission is legal, since 10 meters' phone segment is off-limits to Technician licensees entirely.

Worked example A Technician-class ham keys up a 2-meter FM repeater that's cross-linked to retransmit on 10 meters. Is that legal? It depends entirely on the 10-meter repeater's own control operator: legal if that control operator holds General class or higher, not legal if they're also only Technician class — because the Technician originating station has no 10-meter phone privileges of their own to fall back on, and it's the repeater's control operator's class that has to cover the gap.

Third-party communications, extended

T1F defined third-party communications and the foreign-country agreement requirement. G1E fills in the edges: a third party is disqualified from participating specifically when their own amateur license has been revoked and not reinstated (§97.115(b)(2)) — not for being a non-US citizen, and not for speaking a language other than English; neither of those disqualifies anyone. When a Third-Party Agreement does exist with a foreign country, the message itself is still restricted to matters relating to amateur radio, remarks of a personal character, or emergency/disaster relief (§97.115(a)(2), §97.117) — the same "incidental to the purposes of the Amateur Radio Service" boundary from T1C, just spelled out for the third-party case specifically. And third-party messages may be sent via remote control under exactly the same circumstances they'd otherwise be permitted at all (§97.115) — remote control doesn't add or remove any third-party restriction on its own.

Automatically controlled digital stations

Automatically controlled digital stations sending RTTY or data emissions may communicate with other automatically controlled digital stations on the 6-meter band or any shorter wavelength (higher frequency) band, plus limited segments of some HF bands (§97.221, §97.305) — not "any band segment where digital operation is permitted" broadly; the automatic-to-automatic privilege is narrower than general digital privileges. Reaching a digital station operating under automatic control from outside those specific automatic-control segments requires the initiating station to be under local or remote control — i.e., a human control operator actually present or connected, not another fully automatic station making the call (§97.221).

One narrow but absolute rule: amateur stations may not communicate with unlicensed Wi-Fi stations anywhere in the 2.4 GHz band — no part of that band is open for that kind of cross-service contact (§97.111), regardless of channel.

Finally, a catch-all worth internalizing: interference-avoidance obligations stack, they don't pick one. A licensed operator must take specific steps to avoid harmful interference whenever any of several conditions apply at once — operating within a mile of an FCC monitoring station, using a band where the amateur service is secondary, or transmitting spread spectrum emissions all separately trigger that duty (§97.13(b), §97.303, §97.311(b)).

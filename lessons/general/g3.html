Radio Wave Propagation

Technician's T3 established the basics: the ionosphere bends HF signals back to Earth, VHF/UHF mostly doesn't get that help, and a handful of named mechanisms (sporadic E, tropospheric ducting, meteor scatter) explain VHF/UHF's occasional long hauls. G3 goes deeper into the HF side specifically — not just that the ionosphere bends HF signals, but how much, when, and how to predict it: what drives the ionosphere's day-to-day behavior, and the concrete numbers (MUF, LUF, critical angle) that turn "propagation" from a vibe into something you can reason about before you ever key up.

G3A

Sunspots, solar radiation & geomagnetic indices

The ionosphere is ionized by solar radiation — Technician's T3C already established that much. G3A is about what drives the amount of that ionization, and the numbers hams use to track it day to day.

The sunspot number is the oldest and most familiar indicator: a daily count of visible sunspots, which correlates with how much ionizing ultraviolet radiation the Sun is currently putting out. A higher sunspot number generally means a greater probability of good propagation at higher frequencies — more ionization supports reflecting higher frequencies back to Earth. The count rises and falls on a roughly 11-year solar cycle, and it directly explains a fact worth memorizing: during periods of low solar activity, 15, 12, and 10 meters are the least reliable HF bands for long-distance work — they're the highest-frequency HF bands, so they're the first to lose ionospheric support when the Sun is quiet. Conversely, at essentially any point in the solar cycle — not just at the maximum — 20 meters reliably supports worldwide daytime propagation; it sits low enough in frequency to stay usable even when 15/12/10 meters can't.

The solar flux index (SFI) is a companion number, but measured rather than counted: the intensity of solar radio energy specifically at a 10.7 cm wavelength, tracked because it's a more direct, instrument-based proxy for ionizing output than eyeballing sunspots.

Solar activity doesn't just vary smoothly over the 11-year cycle — it also varies in 26- to 28-day cycles, caused by the Sun's own rotation: since different active regions face Earth as the Sun's surface layers rotate around its axis, propagation conditions cycle with that rotation period.

Worked example Two very different kinds of solar event affect propagation on two very different timescales, and the pool tests both numbers directly. A solar flare's burst of X-ray and ultraviolet radiation travels at the speed of light, so it takes only about 8 minutes to reach Earth — the same 8-minute light-travel time as ordinary sunlight. It causes a sudden ionospheric disturbance (SID), which disrupts lower HF frequencies more than higher ones, by over-ionizing the D region during daylight hours. A coronal mass ejection (CME), by contrast, is physical charged particles — not light — and those particles travel far slower, taking roughly 15 hours to several days to arrive. When they do, they're what typically triggers a geomagnetic storm: a temporary disturbance in Earth's geomagnetic field that degrades HF propagation at high latitudes specifically. A related, steadier source of the same kind of particles — solar wind streaming from coronal holes rather than a single sudden eruption — produces the same basic result: HF communication is disturbed.

Geomagnetic disturbance isn't purely bad news. High geomagnetic activity also strengthens the aurora, and an active aurora can reflect VHF signals — the same auroral backscatter mechanism Technician's T3C introduced, just triggered more often during geomagnetically active periods.

Two numbers quantify geomagnetic disturbance itself, on two different timescales:

K-index

Measures the short-term stability of Earth's geomagnetic field — updated every few hours. Higher means more currently disturbed.

A-index

Measures the long-term stability of Earth's geomagnetic field — a single daily figure, averaged from that day's K-index readings.

G3B

MUF, LUF & short-path/long-path

Every HF path between two specific points has a usable frequency window, bounded on both ends:

MUF — Maximum Usable Frequency

The highest frequency that still refracts back to Earth for that specific path. Go higher and the signal punches through the ionosphere into space instead of coming back down.

LUF — Lowest Usable Frequency

The lowest frequency still practically usable for that same path. Go lower and the D region absorbs the signal before it can reach its destination with usable strength.

Frequencies inside that window — below the MUF and above the LUF — get refracted back to Earth and make the contact possible. Frequencies below the LUF are attenuated before they arrive. Both numbers depend on the same set of factors: path distance and location, time of day and season, and current solar radiation and ionospheric conditions — neither one is a fixed number you look up once.

Worked example Picture the usable window as a range that can widen, narrow, or vanish entirely. During the day, sunlight strengthens the D region, which raises the LUF (more low-frequency absorption) while solar ionization also raises the MUF (more high-frequency support) — the window shifts upward as a whole. If conditions get bad enough that the LUF for a path actually exceeds that path's MUF, the window closes completely: ordinary skywave propagation isn't possible on any frequency over that path, no matter what you try. This is also why operating just below the MUF gives the least attenuation for long-distance skip — you're as far as possible from the LUF's heavy D-region absorption, which is worst at the low end of the window.

Hop distance also depends on which ionospheric region does the refracting — a preview of G3C's region-by-region detail. A single hop off the F2 region (the highest region) covers roughly 2,500 miles; a single hop off the lower E region covers roughly 1,200 miles — about half as far, because the E region sits much closer to the ground, giving the wave a shorter, steeper geometric bounce.

Since propagation conditions are genuinely hard to predict from first principles alone, one practical way to check current propagation on a band from your own station is to use a network of automated internet-connected receiving stations to see where your transmissions are actually being heard — empirical confirmation rather than pure prediction from the K-index or solar flux alone.

Short path and long path are the two great-circle routes between any two points on Earth — the direct way, and the way around the opposite side of the globe. G2D already gave the practical antenna-pointing rule: aim 180° from the short-path heading to attempt long path. Here's the signature that tells you both paths are active on a signal at once: a slightly delayed echo, since the long-path leg of the trip covers far more physical distance at the same speed and so arrives measurably later than the same signal's short-path leg.

Finally, a seasonal noise fact worth knowing alongside all this: the lower HF bands (like 80 and 40 meters) typically show high levels of atmospheric noise or static during summer — a separate nuisance from propagation prediction itself, but one that affects whether a technically "open" band is actually pleasant to operate on.

G3C

Ionospheric regions, critical angle/frequency, scatter & NVIS

Technician's ionosphere entry already named the layers: D, E, and F, in order of increasing altitude. The D region sits closest to the surface of Earth, and it plays a specific, mostly unhelpful role for HF: it's the most absorbent region for signals below 10 MHz during daylight hours, which is exactly why long-distance communication on 40, 60, 80, and 160 meters is noticeably harder during the day — the D region eats those low frequencies on the way up before they ever reach the higher regions that would actually refract them back down. It's also why those same bands routinely open up for distance at night: without sunlight, the D region weakens dramatically and stops getting in the way.

Skip distance depends on which higher region does the actual refracting, and G3B already gave the headline numbers: F2 region skip runs longer (about 2,500 miles per hop) than skip via any other region, precisely because the F2 region is the highest one — a wave bounced off a higher ceiling before returning to Earth covers more ground per hop, the same geometry that makes a basketball bounced off a high wall travel farther than one bounced off a low one.

Two angle-related terms describe the geometric limits of what a given frequency, or a given aiming angle, can do:

Critical frequency

At a given incidence angle, the highest frequency that still gets refracted back to Earth. Go higher in frequency at that same angle and the wave escapes into space.

Critical angle

At a given frequency, the highest takeoff angle that still returns to Earth. Aim any steeper (closer to straight up) at that frequency and the wave escapes.

Those two limits interact directly with the skip zone — the ring-shaped gap between where local ground wave fades out and where the nearest sky-wave hop lands, which normally hears nothing at all. That gap is exactly what NVIS (Near Vertical Incidence Skywave) is built to eliminate: NVIS is short-distance MF or HF propagation that deliberately uses high elevation (takeoff) angles, close to straight up, at frequencies low enough that the critical angle for that frequency is still near 90°. Instead of skipping hundreds of miles away, the signal comes back down close to the transmitter — filling in exactly the regional coverage gap a skip zone would otherwise leave, which is why NVIS is a favorite technique for regional emergency communications nets.

Worked example An emergency-communications net needs reliable coverage across an entire county, at ranges too far for reliable ground wave but too close for a normal skip hop (which would land the signal hundreds of miles beyond the net's own area, inside its own skip zone from the net's perspective). The fix is NVIS: run a low HF frequency with an antenna configuration that radiates mostly straight up, so the "hop" lands just a short distance away instead of skipping over the whole intended coverage area.

Not everything that shows up inside a skip zone is deliberate, though. HF scatter is what lets signals be heard inside a transmitting station's skip zone even without NVIS: a small part of the transmitted energy scatters off ionospheric irregularities and off the Earth's surface into paths that weren't cleanly refracted at all, leaking some signal into a zone that should otherwise be silent. Because only a small fraction of the total energy gets scattered that way, HF scatter signals in the skip zone are typically weak. And because that scattered energy arrives via several different paths simultaneously rather than one clean reflection, HF scatter signals have a characteristic fluttering, distorted sound — the same multiple-paths-combining idea behind multipath from Technician's T3A, here caused by scattering rather than by distinct reflective surfaces.

Amateur Radio Practices

G4 is Technician's T4 grown up for HF: the same idea of wiring a station together and running its controls, but now with tube amplifiers, test gear beyond a basic SWR meter, interference that reaches into your neighbor's TV, and stations that run from a battery in a field instead of a wall outlet. Five groups, sixty questions, mostly concrete facts about equipment you'll actually touch.

G4A

Station configuration and operation

A modern HF transceiver front panel has a handful of controls whose whole job is fighting interference and noise, each in a different, specific way. A notch filter is narrow and tunable: it carves a deep dip out of the receiver passband at one exact frequency, which is exactly the tool for knocking out a steady interfering carrier (a heterodyne whistle) sitting inside an otherwise-clean signal, without touching anything else in the passband. A noise blanker targets a completely different kind of interference — sharp, impulsive noise like ignition pulses or power-line arcing — by watching for those pulses and briefly reducing receiver gain for their duration, so the pulse is muted before it reaches your ears instead of being filtered by frequency at all. And a receiver's noise reduction control, when pushed higher, comes with a real cost: received signals may start to sound distorted, because the processing doing the reducing is working on the wanted signal too, not just the noise around it.

One CW-specific trick uses the receiver itself rather than any dedicated filter: listening on the opposite (reverse) sideband can let you reduce or eliminate interference from another signal, because an interfering signal sitting close to yours on one side of zero beat often lands clear of your signal when you flip which sideband you're listening to. Separately, a receive attenuator solves a different problem entirely: it exists to prevent receiver overload from strong incoming signals by deliberately weakening everything ahead of the receiver's front end, trading some sensitivity to keep a very strong nearby signal from swamping the receiver stages.

Running a vacuum-tube RF power amplifier

Older and higher-power HF amplifiers still commonly use vacuum tubes, tuned by two front-panel controls with two very different jobs. The TUNE control adjusts the amplifier's output tank circuit to resonance at your operating frequency; done correctly, the tell-tale sign is a pronounced dip in plate current — at resonance the tank circuit presents its highest impedance to the tube, so the tube draws the least DC current for a given amount of drive. The LOAD (or COUPLING) control is adjusted differently: the correct setting is whatever gives the desired power output without exceeding the maximum allowable plate current — it's a power/safety trade-off, not a resonance indication like TUNE.

Automatic level control (ALC) is a feedback system that watches the amplifier's output and pulls back the drive automatically to prevent excessive drive — it's what keeps you from overdriving an amplifier into distortion or damage even if your transmitter's own output level creeps up. ALC is helpful for voice, but it has to be switched off for one specific case: transmitting AFSK data signals (RTTY, PSK, and similar audio-tone-based digital modes), because ALC action distorts the signal — digital modes need a transmitter running perfectly linear and constant, and an ALC circuit constantly tugging at the drive level introduces exactly the kind of distortion those modes can't tolerate.

Worked example You bring a tube amplifier up on a new band and slowly advance the TUNE control while watching the plate current meter. The current climbs, then falls, then climbs again as you sweep past the correct setting — where do you stop? At the dip, the low point of plate current, which is where the output tank is resonant at your operating frequency. Only after that do you touch LOADING, adjusting it for the power output you want without pushing plate current past its rated maximum.

Delaying RF output for a moment after a transmitter's keying line activates an external amplifier isn't about the signal at all — it exists to allow time for the amplifier to switch its antenna relay between the transceiver and the amplifier's own output before RF actually arrives, so the relay isn't asked to switch while power is already flowing through it. An electronic keyer (already met on the Technician side) does an unrelated job: automatically generating correctly timed dots and dashes for CW from your paddle motions.

The dual-VFO feature found on most HF transceivers has one very common, practical use: transmitting on one frequency while listening on another — split operation, the standard way to work a rare or high-demand station whose own transmit and receive frequencies are deliberately offset to manage a pileup. Finally, an antenna tuner's purpose, stated precisely for the exam, is to increase power transfer from the transmitter to the feed line — it does this by presenting the transmitter with the impedance it wants to see, even when the antenna system itself is mismatched; it does not lower the SWR out on the antenna side of the feed line, only at the transmitter end.

G4B

Tests and test equipment

An oscilloscope is the one instrument in this group that shows you a signal's actual shape over time rather than a single number: it contains horizontal and vertical channel amplifiers that steer an electron beam (or, on a digital scope, a display trace) across the screen while a voltage moves it up and down, tracing out the waveform itself. That's exactly its advantage over a digital voltmeter: a voltmeter gives you one number, but an oscilloscope lets you see and measure complex waveforms — their shape, timing, and any distortion — not just a single reading. Concretely, that makes an oscilloscope the right tool for checking a CW transmitter's keying waveform (are the dots and dashes clean, or do they click and splatter?), and for viewing a transmitted signal's RF envelope pattern, where the vertical input is connected to the attenuated RF output of the transmitter itself — a sample of the real signal, stepped down to a safe level for the scope's input, not a local oscillator or an external test signal.

Two everyday meters get a "why" behind a design choice each. Voltmeters are built with high input impedance for a specific reason: it decreases the loading on the circuit being measured — a meter that draws significant current of its own would change the very voltage it's trying to read, so a high-impedance meter draws as little current as possible and disturbs the circuit as little as possible. And comparing multimeter types: a digital multimeter's main advantage over an analog one is higher precision in its readings, while an analog meter (the kind with a moving needle) is actually preferred when adjusting a circuit for a maximum or minimum value — watching a needle sweep smoothly toward a peak or a null is easier to follow in real time than watching digits jump around on a digital display.

Worked example You're peaking a receiver's tuned circuit for maximum signal by ear and eye at the same time — why reach for the analog multimeter instead of the digital one sitting right next to it? A digital meter updates in discrete steps and can lag or jitter near a peak, while an analog needle sweeps continuously, making it obvious exactly where the reading stops climbing and starts falling — the point you're actually hunting for.

A two-tone test is the standard way to check a single-sideband transmitter's linearity — whether its amplifying stages reproduce a signal faithfully or distort it. The test signal is two audio tones that aren't harmonically related to each other (not simple multiples of one another); feeding those two tones into the transmitter and examining the RF output on a spectrum analyzer or scope reveals whether the amplifier is generating unwanted intermodulation products — new, unwanted frequencies created by the tones mixing together inside a non-linear stage — which is exactly what excellent linearity avoids.

Two RF-specific instruments round out this group, both already familiar from Technician's T7C but worth restating for General's HF use. A directional wattmeter can determine standing wave ratio, by separately measuring forward and reflected power and computing their ratio. An antenna analyzer needs the antenna and feed line connected to it to make an SWR measurement (not a receiver or transmitter — the analyzer supplies its own low-power test signal), and beyond SWR it can also measure the impedance of coaxial cable. One practical caution: strong signals from nearby transmitters can corrupt an antenna analyzer's readings, specifically by received power interfering with the SWR measurement — the analyzer's detector can't tell its own reflected test signal apart from a strong outside signal landing on the same frequency.

G4C

Interference to consumer electronics; grounding and bonding

This is a different flavor of interference than T7B's receiver overload: here, your transmitted RF is getting into someone else's audio equipment, telephone, or electronics and being demodulated there, not overloading your own receiver. A bypass capacitor at the input of an audio circuit is a common, useful fix — it gives stray RF a low-impedance path to ground while leaving the audio frequencies the circuit actually cares about alone, since a capacitor's opposition to AC drops as frequency rises, so it looks nearly like a short circuit to RF but nearly like an open circuit to audio.

The specific sound this interference makes tells you something about its source. From a single-sideband phone transmitter, RFI getting into an audio device typically comes out as distorted speech — recognizably voice-like, but garbled, because the device is inadvertently demodulating your SSB envelope. From a CW transmitter, it instead comes out as on-and-off humming or clicking, following the rhythm of the dots and dashes rather than sounding like speech at all. Separately, interference that covers a wide range of frequencies at once (rather than one narrow spot) often traces back to arcing at a poor electrical connection — a loose connector or corroded joint sparking under load generates broadband noise, not a signal confined to one frequency.

Worked example A neighbor reports their telephone buzzes only while you're talking on SSB, and the buzzing sounds like distorted speech, not a hum or click. That pattern — distorted speech, keyed by your voice — points straight at SSB envelope demodulation happening somewhere in their phone wiring, not a wideband arcing problem (which wouldn't track your voice) or a CW rig (which would click, not talk).

Grounding, bonding, and RF hot spots

A resonant ground connection is a hazard, not a benefit: if a ground wire happens to be an electrical quarter-wavelength (or some other resonant length) at your operating frequency, it stops behaving like a simple path to ground and instead presents high impedance at that frequency — and high impedance on what's supposed to be your safety ground is exactly what produces high RF voltages on the enclosures of your station equipment, which in turn is a possible cause of RF burns to anyone who touches that equipment. This is also why soldered joints should never be relied on in lightning protection ground connections: a direct lightning strike carries enough heat to melt solder outright, destroying the joint at the exact moment it's needed most — lightning grounds need mechanical, not soldered, connections.

Bonding — tying every piece of station equipment together with low-impedance conductors — is the fix for two related problems General adds on top of T4A's basic definition. It minimizes RF "hot spots" around the station, points where stray RF voltage builds up because some piece of equipment isn't at the same RF potential as everything around it. And it minimizes the effects of a ground loop — an unintended closed loop formed when two pieces of equipment are grounded at two different physical points, so current (including stray RF and hum) can circulate through the loop instead of draining cleanly to a single ground point. A classic symptom of a ground loop in your station's audio wiring is other stations reporting "hum" on your transmitted signal, picked up through that circulating current rather than through your actual audio path.

A more targeted fix applies to common-mode current — unwanted RF current flowing in the same direction on both conductors of a cable (rather than the intended signal current, which flows in opposite directions on the two conductors) — picked up by an audio cable acting as an unintentional antenna. Placing a ferrite choke on the cable adds impedance specifically to that common-mode current without disturbing the wanted signal, which is the standard fix for RFI riding into a station on an audio, USB, or speaker cable. Finally, the reason every metal enclosure of station equipment must be grounded is straightforward safety: it ensures hazardous voltages cannot appear on the chassis if internal insulation fails, giving any fault current a safe path to ground instead of through whoever touches the case.

G4D

Speech processors, S meters & sideband near band edges

A speech processor is an audio compressor built into (or added to) a transceiver, and its purpose is specific: increase the apparent loudness of transmitted voice signals. It does that by compressing the dynamic range of your voice — squeezing quiet and loud parts closer together — which raises the signal's average power without raising its peak power any further (peak power is already capped by your transmitter's rating). More average power in the same peak envelope means a more consistently punchy, readable signal on the receiving end, especially in noisy conditions. Set it wrong, though, and you get real costs: an incorrectly adjusted speech processor can cause distorted speech, excess intermodulation products, and excessive background noise — all three are on the table simultaneously, since over-processing amplifies everything present in the audio, wanted or not.

Reading the S meter

A S meter is a receiver's signal-strength indicator: it measures received signal strength, nothing else (not carrier suppression, not impedance, not your own transmitter power). Its scale is built around decibels: on a properly calibrated meter, one full S unit of change represents roughly 6 dB. Since every 3 dB is a power doubling, 6 dB is two doublings back to back — a factor of about 4× in power. Readings past S9 are labeled directly in dB over S9, and those add the same way: 20 dB over S9 means the signal is 100× more powerful than an S9 signal (10 dB over S9 would be 10×; another 10 dB doubles that factor of ten again, to 100×).

Worked example A distant station's signal reads S8 on your meter. How much must they raise their transmitter power to bring your reading up to S9? One S unit ≈ 6 dB, and 6 dB is two 3 dB (doubling) steps stacked: 2× then 2× again = about 4× the power — not 2×, and nowhere near the 8× you'd get from three doublings.

Sideband and the phone-segment band edge

An SSB signal isn't centered on its displayed "carrier" frequency the way an AM or FM signal is — it occupies a slice of spectrum entirely on one side of that displayed frequency, which is exactly what "sideband" means. A lower sideband (LSB) signal occupies frequencies below the displayed carrier frequency; an upper sideband (USB) signal occupies frequencies above it. A typical voice SSB signal is about 3 kHz wide, so a 3 kHz-wide LSB signal displayed at 7.178 MHz actually occupies 7.175 MHz to 7.178 MHz — the full 3 kHz sits below the displayed number. The mirror image: a 3 kHz-wide USB signal displayed at 14.347 MHz occupies 14.347 MHz to 14.350 MHz, the full 3 kHz sitting above the displayed number.

Worked example You're tuned to 7.178 MHz in LSB with a 3 kHz filter — where does your signal actually sit in the band? LSB occupies the 3 kHz below the displayed frequency: 7.178 MHz − 0.003 MHz = 7.175 MHz to 7.178 MHz. Switch to USB at 14.347 MHz instead, and the signal occupies the 3 kHz above: 14.347 MHz to 14.347 MHz + 0.003 MHz = 14.347 MHz to 14.350 MHz.

That directionality is exactly why operating near a phone segment's band edge needs care, extending T1B's basic band-edge caution to General's wider HF phone privileges: on LSB, since your signal extends downward, your displayed carrier frequency needs to stay at least 3 kHz above the lower edge of the segment, or your sideband energy spills below the edge into spectrum you're not authorized to use. On USB, since your signal extends upward, your displayed carrier needs to stay at least 3 kHz below the upper edge of the segment for the same reason, mirrored.

G4E

Mobile and portable HF stations; alternative energy

A full-size HF antenna won't fit on a vehicle, so mobile HF antennas are physically shortened and then compensated for electrically. A capacitance hat — a small disc or spokes of wire mounted partway up (or at the top of) a mobile whip — exists specifically to electrically lengthen a physically short antenna, letting a whip that's physically much shorter than a quarter wavelength still behave, electrically, closer to one. A corona ball serves a different purpose, mounted right at the tip: it reduces RF voltage discharge from the tip of the antenna while transmitting, by rounding off the sharp point where high RF voltage would otherwise concentrate and arc into the air.

Shortening an antenna isn't free: the main disadvantage of a shortened mobile antenna compared to a full-size one is that its operating bandwidth may be very limited — the loading coils and other tricks used to make a short antenna resonant also make it sharply peaked (high Q), so it works well over only a narrow slice of the band before needing to be retuned. In fact, what most limits an HF mobile installation overall isn't the DC wiring or FCC power rules — it's the efficiency of the electrically short antenna itself; a physically short radiator simply can't radiate as efficiently as a full-size one, no matter how well everything else is set up.

Powering the radio safely extends T4A's mobile-installation advice directly: for a 100-watt HF mobile rig, the best fused power connection runs directly to the battery, using heavy-gauge wire — not to the alternator, and not through insulated "balanced transmission line" (a feed-line concept that doesn't apply to DC power wiring at all). The vehicle's auxiliary power socket (cigarette lighter outlet) is specifically discouraged for a 100-watt transceiver because the socket's wiring may be inadequate for the current the transceiver draws — that wiring was sized for small accessories, not a radio pulling 10+ amps at full output. And once installed, an HF transceiver in a vehicle can pick up receive interference from the battery charging system, the fuel delivery system, and the vehicle's control computers — all three are real, common noise sources in a modern vehicle, not just one of them.

Alternative energy: solar and battery stations

A solar panel is built from many individual photovoltaic cells, and those cells are wired together in a series-parallel configuration: series strings to build up useful voltage (since a single cell's output is small), and multiple strings in parallel to build up useful current. That small single-cell output is worth knowing as a round number: a fully illuminated silicon photovoltaic cell produces an open-circuit voltage of only about 0.5 volts DC — which is exactly why panels need many cells in series to reach a useful charging voltage.

Two protective components matter when a solar panel is paired with a rechargeable battery. A series diode (a "blocking diode") between the panel and the battery exists to prevent the battery from discharging back through the panel during times of low or no illumination — without it, a dark solar panel looks to the battery like just another low-resistance path to drain into overnight. And when connecting a solar panel to a lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4) battery specifically, the panel must have a charge controller in the circuit — a regulator that manages charging current and voltage to match what the battery chemistry can safely accept, since lithium chemistries are far less tolerant of overcharging than lead-acid.

Worked example You're wiring a small solar-charged battery station for portable HF operation and the battery is LiFePO4. Two things belong between the panel and the battery: a series blocking diode (or equivalent function inside the charge controller) so the battery can't drain back through the panel at night, and a charge controller matched to the LiFePO4 chemistry so the battery is never overcharged — skipping either one risks either a dead battery by morning or a damaged one.

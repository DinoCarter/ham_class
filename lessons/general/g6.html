Circuit Components

T6 introduced the basic components — resistor, capacitor, inductor, diode, transistor, rectifier, IC — at a "what is it and what does it do" level. G6 assumes you have that vocabulary already and pushes into the specifics: which kind of capacitor for which job, why a resistor type fails at RF, and two things Technician never touched at all — vacuum tubes and the specialized ICs, connectors, and cores that show up in real HF and VHF gear. Where a term was already covered in Technician, this lesson links to it instead of re-explaining it.

G6A

Resistors, capacitors, inductors, rectifiers, diodes, transistors, vacuum tubes & batteries

Batteries

Two battery facts the pool tests as concrete numbers, not just concepts: for a standard 12-volt lead-acid battery, the minimum allowable discharge voltage for maximum battery life is 10.5 volts — run it down further than that regularly and you shorten its life, even though the battery may still "work" below that point. Separately, a battery's internal resistance matters for a different reason: low internal resistance is what lets a battery deliver high discharge current — the internal resistance is effectively in series with anything you connect, so a lower value means less voltage gets lost internally when you draw a big current, like on transmit.

Diodes: forward threshold voltage

T6B already covered what a diode does (current one direction, blocked the other). G6A adds a specific number for two diode materials: a silicon junction diode has an approximate forward threshold voltage of 0.7 volts, while a germanium diode's is lower, around 0.3 volts. That threshold is the voltage drop across the diode once it's conducting forward — below it, essentially no current flows forward; once you clear it, current starts flowing.

Worked example You're troubleshooting a circuit and measure roughly 0.3 V across a forward-biased diode junction that's clearly conducting. Silicon or germanium? Germanium — its forward threshold voltage runs around 0.3 V, well below silicon's roughly 0.7 V. If you'd measured closer to 0.7 V instead, that would point to a silicon junction.

Capacitors: picking the right type

T6A defined what a capacitor is in general. G6A is about choosing between types for a job: an electrolytic capacitor's defining characteristic is high capacitance for a given physical volume — not tight tolerance, and not unusually low leakage; electrolytics trade precision and leakage performance for raw capacitance density, which is exactly why they show up as big power-supply filter caps rather than precision RF-timing components. A low-voltage ceramic capacitor's defining characteristic, by contrast, is being comparatively low cost — not the tightest tolerance and not the highest stability of the available options, just cheap and good enough for most general uses.

Resistors at RF

A plain resistor opposes current and dissipates heat, as T6A covered. But a wire-wound resistor specifically has a problem at radio frequencies: winding resistance wire into a coil to get a given resistance value also, unavoidably, builds in real inductance — and that inductance's reactance makes the component's actual RF behavior unpredictable, not the simple, frequency-independent resistance you'd want. It's not a tolerance issue or a heat issue; it's that you've accidentally built a small inductor in series with the resistor you meant to have.

Transistors as switches, and MOSFET construction

T6B introduced the bipolar junction transistor and FET generally. Two more specific facts: when a bipolar transistor is used as a switch (fully on or fully off, rather than as a linear amplifier), its two operating points are saturation and cutoff — not the "active region" in between, which is where it behaves as a linear amplifier instead of a switch. And a MOSFET's defining construction detail is that its gate is separated from the channel by a thin insulating layer, rather than by a reverse-biased junction — that insulation is why a MOSFET's gate draws essentially no current at all.

Inductors above self-resonance

Every real inductor has some stray capacitance between its windings, which means it has a self-resonant frequency where that stray capacitance and its intended inductance cancel out. Operate the inductor above that self-resonant frequency and its behavior flips: it stops acting like an inductor at all and becomes capacitive instead — not simply "more inductive" (reactance doesn't just keep climbing) and not a failure mode; it's a predictable consequence of that internal stray capacitance taking over once frequency climbs high enough.

Vacuum tubes

Solid-state components dominate modern gear, but vacuum tubes still show up, especially in high-power RF amplifiers, and General introduces the vocabulary Technician skipped entirely. A vacuum tube's control grid is the element that regulates the flow of electrons between the cathode and the plate — the tube's basic amplifying action, playing the same conceptual role a transistor's base or gate plays in solid-state gear. In a tube with more than one grid, a screen grid serves a specific added purpose: it reduces grid-to-plate capacitance — not primarily to boost efficiency or lower plate resistance, though those can follow indirectly; its direct job is preventing the plate's signal from feeding back into the control-grid circuit through stray capacitance.

Analog & digital ICs, MMICs, display devices, RF connectors & ferrite

ICs: analog, digital, and microwave

T6D's integrated circuit definition covers the general idea (several components combined into one package). G6B sorts ICs into families. An integrated-circuit operational amplifier is an analog device — it processes continuously-varying signals, not discrete digital logic levels. CMOS logic ICs, compared against the older TTL family, have one standout advantage: low power consumption — not higher power handling, and not being better suited to RF amplification or power-supply regulation, which aren't things either logic family is built for in the first place.

An MMIC (Monolithic Microwave Integrated Circuit) is the specialized case: an entire microwave-frequency amplifier or processing stage built onto one chip, rather than assembled from discrete parts — this is what makes compact, inexpensive microwave-band amateur gear (the SHF bands from T1B) practical at all.

Display devices & LEDs

T6B already established that a light-emitting diode is a diode that emits light when current flows through it. G6B confirms the bias direction directly: an LED emits light when it's forward biased — not reverse biased, not at some special "tunnel-effect" region, and not at a Zener breakdown voltage (that's a different diode type used for voltage regulation, not light emission).

Ferrite: cores and beads

A ferrite toroidal inductor has three real advantages working together, which is why the pool's answer to "what's an advantage of a ferrite-core toroid" is all of the following at once: large inductance values can be obtained in a small package, the core's magnetic properties can be optimized for a specific frequency range by choosing the right mix, and (specific to the toroid, donut, shape) most of the magnetic field stays contained within the core instead of radiating stray field into nearby components. What actually determines how a given ferrite core performs at a given frequency isn't its size or the ratio of its outer to inner diameter — it's the composition, or "mix," of materials the core is made from; different mixes are formulated for different frequency ranges.

A ferrite bead or core slipped over a coaxial cable's shield reduces unwanted common-mode current (current flowing the same direction on the outside of the shield, instead of cancelling as intended) by a specific mechanism: creating an impedance in that current's path — not by converting it to differential-mode current, not by generating a cancelling out-of-phase current, and ferrites don't "expel" magnetic fields the way a superconductor would. It's a straightforward added series impedance, positioned specifically where only the unwanted common-mode current flows.

Worked example You're getting RF feedback into a microphone cable running near your antenna feed line. Clamping a ferrite core around the mic cable is supposed to help — by what mechanism? The ferrite adds impedance specifically in the path of common-mode current (RF current picked up identically on both conductors of the cable, flowing back toward your equipment). It doesn't cancel the RF with an opposing signal or convert it to some other current type; it just makes that particular current's path harder to complete, so less of it makes it back to the radio.

G8A

Carriers & modulation

Modulation always means the same thing at its core: varying some property of a carrier to encode information onto it. The three classic analog modes each vary a different property:

Amplitude modulation (AM)

Varies the carrier's instantaneous power level (its amplitude) to carry information — louder audio makes a bigger swing in signal strength.

Frequency modulation (FM)

Varies the carrier's instantaneous frequency to carry information — the carrier's power stays constant, only where it sits on the dial wobbles.

Phase modulation (PM) is the third classic mode: it varies the carrier's phase angle rather than its frequency or amplitude. PM and FM are close cousins in practice — a reactance modulator connected to a transmitter's RF amplifier stage is, specifically, what produces phase modulation, and PM's varying phase looks enough like varying frequency that PM signals are routinely received on an ordinary FM receiver.

Digital modes vary a fourth property, keeping the same basic idea: direct binary FSK (frequency-shift keying) is generated by changing an oscillator's frequency directly with a digital control signal — a "1" bit and a "0" bit each simply push the oscillator to a different frequency, with no audio tone involved at all.

The modulation envelope, and what goes wrong

An AM signal's modulation envelope is the waveform traced out by connecting the peak values of the modulated signal over time — picture drawing a line across just the tops of the RF waveform's peaks; that traced line is the envelope, and it's shaped exactly like the original audio that was used to modulate the carrier.

Worked example Talk too loudly into an AM or SSB microphone and the transmitter can't track the envelope anymore — the drive or speech level simply exceeds what the stage can faithfully reproduce, and the top of the envelope gets clipped flat instead of following the audio's actual peaks. That specific distortion has a name: flat-topping. The more general symptom this falls under is overmodulation, and its practical effect on the air is excessive bandwidth — the clipped, distorted envelope splatters energy well outside the channel the signal should occupy, the same real-world consequence as T7B's overdrive, just described here at the modulation-theory level instead of the troubleshooting level.

Among the phone emissions the pool compares, SSB uses the narrowest bandwidth — consistent with T8A's bandwidth table, and the direct payoff of removing the carrier and one whole sideband that a full AM signal would otherwise carry.

Digital phase modulation: PSK & QPSK

PSK encodes data as carrier phase shifts. QPSK (Quadrature PSK) is a specific PSK variant that transmits digital data using 0-, 90-, 180-, and 270-degree phase shifts to represent pairs of bits at once, rather than PSK31's single bit per phase shift — four possible phase states instead of two, so each individual phase shift carries twice the information. QPSK31 applies that same trick to a PSK31-style signal: it's sideband-sensitive (which sideband you're tuned to matters, unlike plain BPSK31), its encoding provides error correction that plain PSK31 doesn't have, and despite carrying twice the data per phase shift, its bandwidth comes out approximately the same as BPSK31's.

FT8, already introduced as a low-signal-to-noise digital mode, uses a specific modulation to get there: 8-tone frequency shift keying — eight distinct audio tones, each representing a different combination of bits, cycled through at a fixed, precisely-timed rate.

Link budget & link margin

Two related numbers describe whether a link (say, to a distant satellite or weak-signal contact) will actually close. A link budget is the running total: transmit power plus antenna gains, minus every system loss along the way, as seen at the receiver — add up everything helping the signal and subtract everything hurting it, and what's left is the signal level that actually arrives. Link margin then compares that arriving signal against what the receiver actually needs: it's the difference between the received power level and the minimum signal level required at the receiver's input. A healthy positive link margin means there's room to spare before the link drops out; a shrinking margin is an early warning that a link is close to unreadable.

G8B

Frequency changing, bandwidth, deviation & intermodulation

A mixer converts a signal from one frequency to another by combining it with a second signal and outputting the sum and difference of the two — already familiar from T7A. G8B names the pieces precisely: the mixer input that's varied or tuned to actually perform that conversion is the local oscillator (LO); the fixed frequency the incoming signal gets converted to, where the receiver's filters and demodulator actually do their work, is the intermediate frequency (IF). Heterodyning is simply another name for this same mixing process — not a different technique, just a different word for it.

That frequency-conversion trick has a specific failure mode worth naming: an image response is interference from an unwanted signal sitting at twice the IF frequency away from the desired signal — the mixer can't tell that image-frequency signal apart from the one you actually wanted, so both land on the same IF and both come through. Good receiver design (extra front-end filtering, a well-chosen IF) is what keeps image response from being a practical problem.

Frequency conversion isn't only for receivers. A VHF FM transmitter often reaches its final operating frequency with a multiplier stage — a stage that deliberately generates a harmonic of a lower starting frequency, then filters out everything except that one harmonic to use as the actual transmit frequency. It's the same physical phenomenon T7B treated as an unwanted spurious emission, here put to deliberate, controlled use instead.

Deviation & bandwidth, with real numbers

Worked example What's the total bandwidth of an FM phone signal with 5 kHz deviation and a 3 kHz modulating (audio) frequency? Add deviation and modulating frequency, then double it: (5 kHz + 3 kHz) × 2 = 16 kHz. That "add them, then double" rule is the standard way the pool wants FM bandwidth estimated — both the deviation and the highest audio frequency being sent push the sidebands outward, and they do it on both sides of the carrier.
Worked example A 146.52 MHz FM phone transmitter is built around a 12.21 MHz reactance-modulated oscillator, multiplied up to the final transmit frequency, with 5 kHz of deviation at the final output. What's the deviation actually happening back at that 12.21 MHz oscillator stage? First find the multiplication factor: 146.52 MHz ÷ 12.21 MHz = 12 exactly. Frequency multiplication scales deviation by that same factor, so working backward, the oscillator's own deviation is 5,000 Hz ÷ 12 ≈ 416.7 Hz — a twelfth of the final deviation, because every later multiplier stage that reaches the final frequency multiplies that small starting deviation right along with the frequency itself.

Two related facts about mode bandwidth and receiver setup: transmitted symbol rate and bandwidth are directly linked — higher symbol rates (more symbols per second) require wider bandwidth, since packing more information into each second of transmission means the signal has to change state faster, and faster state changes occupy more spectrum. And matching a receiver's bandwidth to the bandwidth of whatever mode it's actually receiving is good practice because it results in the best signal-to-noise ratio: a receiver bandwidth wider than the signal it's demodulating just lets in extra noise that a narrower, well-matched filter would have excluded.

Duty cycle is already familiar from T0C's RF-exposure math; G8B tests a completely different reason it matters: some operating modes run at high duty cycles that could exceed a transmitter's average power rating, even if the peak power never exceeds the radio's limits — a mode that transmits continuously (like FT8 or RTTY) stresses a transmitter's output stage in a way that a mode with lots of natural pauses (like SSB voice) doesn't, purely because of how much of the time it's actually keying the amplifier.

Intermodulation, revisited

T7B introduced intermodulation as two or more signals mixing in a nonlinear stage to create phantom frequencies. G8B adds the actual math: mixing two signals at frequencies F1 and F2 in a nonlinear circuit produces intermodulation products at combinations like 2F1−F2, 2F2−F1, 3F1−2F2, and so on. The odd-order products — the ones whose coefficients add up to an odd number, like 2F1−F2 (coefficients 2 and 1, summing to an odd total when you count the sign) — land closest to the original two frequencies, which is exactly why they're the most troublesome in practice: they're the hardest to filter away from the signals you actually want.

Worked example Two signals sit at F1 = 14.200 MHz and F2 = 14.210 MHz, just 10 kHz apart. The odd-order product 2F1−F2 works out to (2 × 14.200) − 14.210 = 14.190 MHz — only 10 kHz away from F1 itself, right in the middle of the same crowded band the original two signals occupy. That's the concrete reason odd-order intermodulation is such a headache: the phantom product doesn't land safely out of band, it lands practically on top of the signals that created it.

G8C

Digital emission modes

T8D inventoried digital modes at the "what is it for" level; G8C goes one layer deeper into how several of them actually encode and protect data.

Packet radio's header (the part carrying routing and handling information, including the destination call sign) is already familiar. Two older data codes come up by name: Baudot code is a 5-bit code with additional start and stop bits added around each character, historically used for RTTY. Varicode is the different scheme PSK31 uses instead: unlike Baudot's fixed 5 bits per character, Varicode assigns shorter bit sequences to common letters and longer sequences to rarer ones — which is specifically why, in PSK31, upper-case letters (rarer than lower-case in ordinary text) use longer Varicode sequences and therefore transmit slower than the same text in lower case.

Worked example You're running an ARQ (automatic repeat request) data link and a packet arrives corrupted. The receiving station's reply is a NAK (negative acknowledgment) — its specific meaning is a request to retransmit that packet, the ARQ system's way of saying "that one didn't make it, send it again" without any operator intervention. If that request-and-resend cycle keeps failing after repeated attempts, the practical result isn't an infinite retry loop: the connection simply drops.

Forward error correction (FEC) takes a different approach to the same underlying problem — instead of detecting an error and asking for a resend (ARQ's approach), FEC lets the receiver correct errors directly by transmitting redundant information along with the data in the first place, so an error can often be fixed on the spot without any retransmission at all.

WSPR is a specific, narrow, low-power digital mode used as a beacon for assessing HF propagation — stations around the world run it continuously at very low power specifically to map out which paths are actually open at a given moment, rather than for two-way conversation.

A Frequency Shift Keyed (FSK) signal's two states are conventionally called mark and space — not "dot and dash" (that's CW) and not simply "on and off" (that's a different kind of keying entirely); FSK is always transmitting one tone or the other, never silence.

Waterfall displays & watching your own signal

A waterfall display — the scrolling spectrum view most digital-mode software puts front and center — puts frequency on the horizontal axis, signal strength shown as intensity (brightness or color), and time running down the vertical axis, with the newest data usually at the top and older data scrolling down and off the bottom. One specific thing to watch for on it: one or more extra vertical lines appearing on either side of your own data or RTTY signal indicate overmodulation — you're driving your transmitter too hard and splattering energy outward, visible directly as those unwanted sidebar lines.

An FT8 signal report like "+3" is a specific, defined number, not a loose estimate: it means the signal-to-noise ratio is equivalent to +3 dB measured in a 2.5 kHz bandwidth — a standardized reference bandwidth that lets every FT8 report mean the same thing regardless of what receive bandwidth the reporting station actually used.

Finally, a quick vocabulary check on what counts as what: DMR, D-STAR, and System Fusion are digital voice modes — already familiar in part from DMR's talkgroups and D-STAR's call-sign-based identification — distinct from data-only digital modes like FT8, WSPR, and PACTOR. Mesh network microwave nodes add one more resilience property worth knowing: if one node fails, a packet can often still reach its destination via an alternate node, the same kind of automatic re-routing that makes any mesh network more resilient than a single point-to-point link.

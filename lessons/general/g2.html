Operating Procedures

G1 was the law that comes with your new HF privileges. G2 is what you actually do with them: which sideband to use and why it isn't arbitrary, how to break into a contact without stepping on anyone, the etiquette that keeps a crowded HF band usable, the CW shorthand that predates voice operating by decades, who's watching for rule violations, and how the newer digital modes expect you to behave. Technician's T2 covered VHF/UHF etiquette; G2 is the HF-specific layer on top of it.

G2A

Phone operating: sidebands, breaking in & voice setup

Technician's SSB entry already explained the mechanics: single sideband strips a full AM signal down to one sideband and drops the carrier, saving bandwidth and transmitter power for the same intelligible audio. What Technician didn't need to cover is that SSB comes in two mirror-image flavors — upper sideband (USB) and lower sideband (LSB) — and which one you use is governed by a band-by-band convention, not a technical requirement. Get it backwards and the other station's radio can often still partially decode you (garbled, shifted-pitch audio), which is exactly the symptom that gives away a sideband mismatch.

The convention splits cleanly at 14 MHz:

Band(s) Sideband
160, 75/80, 40 metersLSB
20, 17, 15, 12, 10 meters (14 MHz and up)USB
Every VHF/UHF SSB bandUSB

Why lower sideband specifically below 14 MHz? Not efficiency, not legality, not compatibility with an AM detector — the honest answer the exam pool gives is that it's commonly accepted amateur practice. The split traces back to early SSB adoption decades ago and simply stuck as convention; every modern transceiver just bakes the table above in as a default per band so you never have to think about it. It's worth knowing anyway, because if you ever manually override your radio's sideband selection, this is the table to check against.

Worked example You're setting up a 20-meter phone contact and a 75-meter phone contact back to back. Your radio should be in USB for the 20-meter contact (14 MHz and up) and LSB for the 75-meter one — the exact opposite settings, on two bands you might work minutes apart. Most radios switch this automatically based on the dial frequency, but it's the reason a manually-forced wrong sideband is a classic troubleshooting culprit when a contact suddenly sounds unintelligible.

Breaking into a contact that's already underway between two other stations follows a different rule than calling CQ cold. The recommended way: during a natural pause, say your call sign once. Not "QRZ" repeated, not "Breaker breaker" (that's CB radio convention, not amateur practice), and not a CQ aimed at either station — a single, clean call sign is enough to signal you're waiting, and it's brief enough not to compound the interruption if your timing was slightly off.

Two mechanical facts about voice transmitter setup round out this group. VOX (voice-operated switch) and PTT (push-to-talk) both get you on the air, but they solve different problems: PTT requires you to physically hold a button down while talking, while VOX switches the transceiver to transmit automatically whenever it hears your voice, which is what makes hands-free operation possible in the first place — useful if your hands are busy with a logbook, a mic-adjacent task, or a mobility limitation. And on an SSB transceiver, ALC (Automatic Level Control) is the circuit protecting your transmitted audio from distortion when you talk too loudly; the control you actually adjust to keep ALC happy is your transmit audio or microphone gain, not power output or antenna tuning.

Finally, "CQ DX" from a station inside the contiguous 48 states carries an implicit scope: it means the caller is specifically listening for any station outside the lower 48 — Alaska, Hawaii, or another country all qualify. Answering a CQ DX call from inside the lower 48 states (when you're also inside the lower 48) isn't following the convention the caller intended.

G2B

Operating effectively: band plans, courtesy & RACES

One rule anchors frequency access on HF, and it echoes the priority rule Technician's T2C established for VHF nets: except during genuine emergencies, no amateur station has priority access to any frequency (§97.101(b),(c)). Not nets, not an ongoing QSO, not a contest — nobody "owns" a frequency simply by having used it first. If propagation shifts mid-contact and another station starts sharing your frequency, the expected practice is exactly the T2B pattern already familiar from Technician: negotiate a mutually acceptable resolution with the other station, not invoke a priority claim that doesn't exist.

If a station in actual distress breaks into your contact, the first thing to do is acknowledge them and determine what assistance is needed — not immediately go silent, not cut power, not escalate to a coordinator before you even know what's wrong. Figuring out the need comes before anything else.

Before calling CQ on an apparently clear frequency, confirm it's actually clear: on CW, send "QRL?" followed by your call sign; on phone, ask if the frequency is in use, followed by your call sign. This is the HF-appropriate version of the courtesy Technician learned as the reverse-function check on a repeater input — here there's no repeater to check against, so you ask directly. Once you're transmitting, minimum separation from adjacent signals matters too: roughly 150–500 Hz between CW signals, and roughly 2–3 kHz between SSB signals — SSB needs more room because a voice signal occupies far more bandwidth than a CW carrier's narrow shift.

Worked example You tune across the 40-meter CW band looking for a place to call CQ and find a signal every 300–400 Hz or so. Since the minimum CW separation is roughly 150–500 Hz, squeezing in right next to one of those signals would cause interference — you need to find an actually clear gap, not just the nearest quiet -sounding spot.

Beyond the hard rule in §97.101, a band plan is the same voluntary, amateur-coordinated layer Technician's T2A introduced — nothing in it is individually FCC-enforceable, but following it is commonly accepted practice and the standard the pool expects. One specific HF/6-meter example worth knowing by number: the 50.1–50.125 MHz segment is voluntarily reserved, for stations transmitting within the 48 contiguous states, for contacts with stations outside the 48 contiguous states — a DX-only window, by convention rather than regulation.

Good net-management practice adds one more item beyond what T2C already covered: keeping a backup frequency in case interference or poor propagation disrupts the primary one, so the net has somewhere to go without falling apart.

RACES reappears here with two specific operational details Technician's T1/T2C didn't need. First: a RACES control operator, even one assisting relief operations during an actual disaster, must still be a person holding an FCC-issued amateur operator license (§97.407(a)) — a government official alone, without an amateur license, cannot serve as control operator just because the traffic is disaster-related. Second: RACES training drills and tests may be conducted routinely, without special authorization, for no more than 1 hour per week (§97.407(d)(4)) — a cap meant to keep "practice" traffic from crowding out real operating on RACES-designated frequencies.

G2C

CW operating procedures, Q signals & full break-in

Technician's Q signal entry covered the concept: three-letter Morse-era shorthand, all starting with Q, standing in for common phrases so operators don't have to spell out full sentences letter by letter. G2C adds the specific Q signals and CW procedural conventions the General pool actually tests:

QRL?

"Are you busy?" / "Is this frequency in use?" — the question you send before calling CQ on an apparently clear CW frequency.

QRS?

A request to slow down. If a station sends you "QRS?", the correct response is to send more slowly.

QRN

"I am troubled by static" — a report about atmospheric noise, not a request to change anything about the other station's sending.

QRV

"I am ready to receive" — an announcement of readiness, similar in spirit to a repeater's "listening" from T2A but in Q-signal form.

QSL

"I have received and understood" — an acknowledgment of correct copy, not a claim that a contact has already been confirmed by card or log.

Two non-Q procedural signals matter too: sending "KN" at the end of a transmission means you're listening only for a specific station or stations — an exclusive invitation, unlike a general CQ. And the prosign "AR" marks the end of a formal message when using CW, distinct from other sign-off prosigns used at different points in a contact.

Two more CW-specific mechanics: when answering someone else's CQ, send at the fastest speed you're comfortable copying, but no faster than the CQ itself was sent — matching or undershooting their speed, never exceeding it, so you don't invite a reply faster than you can actually copy. And "zero beat" means precisely matching your transmit frequency to the frequency of a signal you just received, so your reply lands exactly where the other station is listening rather than slightly off to one side. One more reporting detail: adding a "C" to an RST signal report means the signal sounded chirpy or unstable — a note about the transmitted signal's quality, not about copy percentage or key clicks.

Worked example You're mid-QSO on CW and want to let the other station finish their thought without worrying that anyone else is trying to break in. Ending your transmission with "KN" tells them — and anyone else listening — that you're listening only for that specific station's reply, not opening the frequency to a general call.

Full break-in (QSK) is the CW-specific analog of duplex-style responsiveness: the transmitting station's receiver re-engages fast enough to hear the other station's signal between individual code characters and elements, not just between whole transmissions. That's a meaningfully finer grain than an operator flipping a manual send/receive switch before and after each transmission (ordinary "semi" break-in) — QSK lets someone interrupt you mid-word, and you'd actually hear it happen.

G2D

The Volunteer Monitor Program & HF operating practice

Don't confuse this with Volunteer Examiners. The Volunteer Monitor Program is a separate corps of amateur volunteers, formally enlisted specifically to monitor the airwaves for rules violations — they don't administer license exams, coordinate repeaters, or run emergency nets; that's VEs, Frequency Coordinators, and ARES/RACES respectively. Its stated objective is to encourage amateur operators to self-regulate and comply with the rules, reinforcing good practice rather than functioning primarily as an enforcement arm. One specific Volunteer Monitor technique worth knowing: to localize a station whose continuous, unwanted carrier is holding a repeater's squelch open (denying everyone else access), Volunteer Monitors in different locations compare beam headings on the repeater's input frequency from their own homes — the same triangulation idea behind radio direction finding, applied cooperatively across multiple fixed observers instead of one mobile hunter.

That triangulation idea connects to a mapping tool worth knowing by name: an azimuthal projection map is a map centered on one specific location that shows true bearings and distances from that location to anywhere else on it — exactly what you'd want on hand to point a directional antenna, or to plot beam headings gathered from several fixed monitoring stations.

Worked example Several Volunteer Monitors each note the compass bearing at which a troublesome continuous carrier is strongest on their own directional antenna, aimed at the repeater's input frequency. Plotted as lines from each monitor's home location on an azimuthal map, those bearings converge roughly where the offending station is — no single monitor needs to physically drive toward the signal.

Calling CQ for a general HF contact follows the same pattern Technician's T2A already established for VHF: repeat "CQ" a few times, say "this is," then your call sign a few times, then pause to actually listen before repeating. Nothing about HF changes that convention.

Long-path propagation gets a concrete pointing rule: to attempt a long-path contact, aim a directional antenna 180° from the station's short-path heading — that is, point the "wrong" way around the compass on purpose, toward the great-circle route that goes the long way around the globe instead of the direct way. (G3B expands on why both paths can carry a signal at once.)

A few more HF operating habits worth internalizing: many amateurs keep a station log primarily to have a record on hand if the FCC ever requests information about their station's operation — not because Part 97 requires logging routine domestic contacts. During an HF contest, the one hard requirement is unchanged from every other kind of operating: identify your station according to normal FCC regulations. And signal reports are conventionally exchanged at the very start of an HF contact specifically so each station can adjust how it operates to match conditions — power, speed, mode choices — rather than as a formality for award programs or logging.

Finally, QRP operation means deliberately transmitting with low power — a self-imposed challenge and efficiency exercise some operators pursue on HF, not a licensing category or regulatory limit.

G2E

Digital mode operating procedure

This group isn't about what the digital modes are — Technician's T8D already introduced the mode vocabulary. It's about the operating conventions specific to using them well: where on the band, which sideband, how timing works, and how to troubleshoot when decoding fails.

Start with sideband, because it splits two ways depending on the mode — a direct echo of the USB/LSB convention from G2A, applied to AFSK (Audio Frequency-Shift Keying, where digital tones are fed into an SSB transmitter's microphone input rather than shifting the radio's oscillator directly):

RTTY via AFSK

Conventionally sent on LSB — the older convention, inherited from RTTY's history alongside voice LSB use on the lower HF bands.

JT65 / JT9 / FT4 / FT8 via AFSK

Conventionally sent on USB — the newer WSJT-X-family convention, regardless of which HF band you're on.

Worked example You switch from working RTTY to working FT8 on the same HF band without touching your radio's sideband setting. Your FT8 signal comes out garbled because your radio is still in LSB (correct for RTTY) instead of USB (what FT8 expects). Two digital modes, both AFSK, both routinely used on the same bands — but opposite sideband conventions.

RTTY specifics worth knowing: the most common frequency shift between the "mark" and "space" tones on amateur HF RTTY is 170 Hz. And if you can't decode an RTTY or other FSK signal even though it appears to be tuned in properly, there are several independent possible causes that all produce the same symptom — the mark and space frequencies could be reversed, you might have the wrong baud rate selected, or you might be listening on the wrong sideband. As with the T2B repeater-access troubleshooting example, one symptom doesn't tell you which cause it is; you check all three.

Frequency and timing discipline matter more on digital modes than on phone or CW, because many of them (like FT8) are structured around precise, fixed time slots rather than free-form back-and-forth. Two consequences:

  • When answering a station calling CQ on FT8, good practice is to find a clear frequency during the alternate time slot to the calling station — not on the calling station's exact frequency, and not in the same time slot, which would collide with their own next transmission.
  • FT8 in particular requires computer clock time accurate to within roughly one second, since the software uses precise timing to know which time slot it's currently in and to decode signals that arrive right at a slot boundary.

Band segments matter too: on 20 meters, most digital mode activity clusters between 14.070 MHz and 14.100 MHz, with FT8 specifically concentrated in a narrow slice around 14.074–14.077 MHz within that range.

Two connection-oriented digital protocols behave differently from broadcast-style modes like FT8. PACTOR and VARA both establish a direct connection between exactly two stations — PACTOR in particular has no mechanism for a third station to "join" an existing link the way you might join an open net; you simply can't. Both are commonly used to carry Winlink traffic over RF. When either link suffers interference from other signals, the symptoms can show up several different ways at once — frequent retries or timeouts, long pauses in message transmission, or outright failure to establish the connection in the first place — because interference can strike any part of the protocol's handshaking.

To establish contact with a gateway station running a digital messaging system, the way in is to transmit a connect message on that station's published frequency — there's no broadcast SSID to listen for or email to send first; the gateway's frequency is the entry point, publicized in advance the same way a repeater's frequency and offset are. A Winlink Remote Message Server is simply another name for this kind of gateway station in the Winlink system specifically.

Last, one non-HF digital system worth knowing by name and purpose: an AREDN (Amateur Radio Emergency Data Network) mesh network exists primarily to provide high-speed data services during an emergency or community event — built from commercial Wi-Fi hardware running amateur firmware, the way Technician's T8D mesh network entry describes, but purpose-built for exactly this use case.

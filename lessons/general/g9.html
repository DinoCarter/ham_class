G9A

Feed lines: characteristic impedance, attenuation & SWR calculation

T9B named a feed line's characteristic impedance without deriving it. For an open, parallel-conductor line (like ladder line or "window line"), that impedance is set by geometry alone: the distance between the centers of the two conductors, and the radius of the conductors themselves — wider spacing relative to conductor thickness raises the impedance, closer spacing lowers it. Window line's specific geometry lands it at a nominal 450 ohms, a long way from coax's familiar 50 ohms — which is exactly why the two feed line types need completely different matching hardware and can't just be swapped in for each other.

Why mismatches happen, and what they cost

Reflected power at an antenna's feed point has one specific cause: a difference between the feed line's characteristic impedance and the antenna's actual feed point impedance. Whatever power that mismatch reflects travels back down the line toward the transmitter instead of radiating — which is exactly the condition SWR measures. Preventing standing waves in the first place comes down to that same single requirement: the antenna feed point impedance has to be matched to the feed line's characteristic impedance.

Coax attenuation increases with frequency (already familiar from T9B), and it's conventionally expressed in decibels per 100 feet — a manufacturer's spec sheet listing loss "per 100 ft" at a given frequency is giving you exactly that figure.

Worked example: calculating SWR SWR compares a load's actual impedance to the feed line's characteristic impedance, always expressed as the larger number over the smaller: take whichever of (load ÷ line) or (line ÷ load) comes out above 1. Connect a 50-ohm feed line to a 200-ohm resistive load: 200 ÷ 50 = 4, so the result is 4:1 SWR. Connect that same 50-ohm line to a 10-ohm resistive load instead: now the line's impedance is the larger number, 50 ÷ 10 = 5, giving 5:1 SWR. Same feed line, two different mismatches, two different ratios — and either direction of mismatch (load too high or too low relative to the line) makes SWR climb away from the ideal 1:1.

One counterintuitive wrinkle, and one common troubleshooting trap:

  • High SWR makes feed line loss worse — on an already-lossy line, a mismatch increases the loss beyond what a well-matched line of the same length and type would have.
  • But feed line loss reduces the SWR you actually measure at the transmitter end of the line, compared to the true SWR sitting at the antenna. The reflected wave has to travel back down the same lossy line a second time, so it arrives back at the transmitter weaker than it left the antenna — which makes the measured mismatch look better than it really is. A long, lossy run can hide a genuinely bad antenna match from a meter sitting at the shack end of the line.
Worked example Your antenna feed point shows 5:1 SWR. You insert a matching network (an antenna tuner) right at the transmitter, and it adjusts to present a clean 1:1 SWR to the transmitter itself. What's the SWR out on the actual feed line, between the tuner and the antenna? Still 5:1, unchanged — the tuner only fixes what the transmitter "sees" at its own output; it does nothing to the mismatch physically sitting out at the antenna, and the feed line between the tuner and the antenna keeps carrying exactly the same standing waves (and the same extra loss) it had before the tuner was ever connected.

G9B

Basic dipole & monopole antennas

T9A already covered a half-wave dipole's basic shape and its broadside radiation pattern. G9B fills in the real numbers and the geometry behind that pattern. In free space, a dipole's radiation pattern in the plane containing the wire is a figure-eight, at right angles to the antenna — two lobes, one off each broadside direction, and essentially nothing straight off either end, matching T9A's "radiates broadside" description exactly.

Worked example: dipole & monopole length A half-wave dipole's approximate length in feet is 468 ÷ frequency in MHz. Cut for 14.250 MHz: 468 ÷ 14.250 ≈ 33 feet. Cut for a lower frequency, 3.550 MHz: 468 ÷ 3.550 ≈ 132 feet — a lot more wire for a lot lower frequency, exactly as T9A's "shorter antenna, higher resonant frequency" rule predicts. A quarter-wave monopole uses almost exactly half that dipole formula, 234 ÷ frequency in MHz: cut for 28.5 MHz, that's 234 ÷ 28.5 ≈ 8 feet.

Height and feed position both move a dipole's feed point impedance around, in opposite directions:

  • Lowering a horizontal half-wave dipole toward 1/10 wavelength above ground steadily decreases its feed point impedance — the nearby ground increasingly loads the antenna down the closer it gets.
  • Moving the feed point from the center of a half-wave dipole toward either end steadily increases the feed point impedance instead — center feed sits at the dipole's lowest-impedance point, and impedance climbs steeply as you move toward the high-impedance ends.
  • Antenna height also reshapes the azimuthal pattern, not just its impedance: for a horizontal dipole at elevation angles above about 45°, if the antenna sits lower than 1/2 wavelength above ground, the azimuthal pattern becomes almost omnidirectional at those high angles — the sharp figure-eight pattern is really a lower-elevation-angle phenomenon that a low, close-to-ground dipole doesn't cleanly reproduce straight up.

Polarization has a real, measurable consequence on HF, not just a "match your polarization" rule of thumb: a horizontally polarized HF antenna has an advantage over a vertically polarized one in lower ground losses — a vertical antenna's radiation travels close to the (lossy, imperfectly conducting) ground for a longer path than a horizontal antenna's does, so more of a vertical signal's power gets absorbed by the ground itself before it ever leaves the near field.

Two specific antenna types round out the group. A random-wire HF antenna — just a wire of no particular resonant length, connected straight to the transmitter with no tuner-side isolation — has a real practical downside: the station equipment itself may end up carrying significant RF current, since without a proper matching network the "ground" side of the antenna system routes back through the radio and its cabling. An elevated quarter-wave ground-plane vertical radiates omnidirectionally in azimuth (equally in every horizontal direction, the vertical counterpart to a dipole's figure-eight), and a common way to adjust its feed point impedance toward the usual 50 ohms is simply to slope its radials downward from the horizontal rather than leaving them flat — the steeper the downward slope, the lower the feed point impedance drops. For a ground-mounted vertical instead, the radial wires belong right at ground level: on the surface, or buried just a few inches down.

G9C

Directional antennas

T9A introduced the Yagi as the amateur beam antenna with the greatest gain. G9C looks at what actually controls that gain, and the vocabulary for describing its directional pattern precisely.

A three-element Yagi's driven element — the one actually connected to the feed line — runs about 1/2 wavelength long, the same as an ordinary dipole. The other two elements are each tuned slightly off that length in a specific direction: the reflector (behind the driven element, away from the favored direction) is longer than the driven element, and the director (in front, toward the favored direction) is shorter — those length differences are exactly what makes the whole array radiate more strongly in one direction instead of equally in all of them.

Several physical parameters can be tuned to improve a Yagi's performance, and they aren't interchangeable: larger-diameter elements specifically increase the antenna's bandwidth (a fatter element presents a less sharply-tuned reactance across frequency), while the boom length, the number of elements on it, and the spacing between them all can be adjusted to optimize forward gain, front-to-back ratio, or SWR bandwidth — there's no single "the" adjustment; a Yagi's real-world design is a set of tradeoffs across all of these dimensions at once. The single biggest lever for gain specifically is adding more directors and lengthening the boom to hold them: more elements spread over a longer boom is the primary way a Yagi's gain increases.

Main lobe

The direction of maximum radiated field strength from a directive antenna — literally, "where the beam points."

Front-to-back ratio

The power radiated in the main lobe compared to the power radiated in the exact opposite direction — how well the antenna suppresses signal (and interference) coming from behind it.

Worked example: dBi vs. dBd, and stacking gain An antenna's gain is often quoted two ways: dBd (relative to a reference dipole) and dBi (relative to a theoretical isotropic radiator). A plain dipole itself already has 2.15 dB of gain over isotropic, so for the same physical antenna, its gain expressed in dBi always reads 2.15 dB higher than the same gain expressed in dBd — nothing about the antenna changed, just which reference point the number is measured against. Stacking gain works differently: take two identical three-element Yagis, horizontally polarized, spaced a half-wavelength apart vertically, and feed them together — the combined gain comes out only about 3 dB higher than a single Yagi alone, not the full 6 dB doubling the power going into two antennas might suggest. The stack isn't perfectly efficient at combining the two antennas' fields, so real-world stacking gain lands well under the theoretical maximum.

Two named impedance-matching techniques specific to Yagis round out the group. A beta match (or hairpin match) is a shorted transmission-line stub placed right at a Yagi's feed point to handle impedance matching. A gamma match takes a different approach, and its defining characteristic is that it doesn't require the driven element to be insulated from the boom — unlike some other matching schemes, a gamma match can connect straight to a driven element that's electrically bonded to a grounded metal boom.

G9D

Specialized antenna types & applications

A handful of antennas exist to solve one specific job well, rather than to be general-purpose. Each of the following trades away all-around usefulness for real performance at its one intended task.

NVIS

For short-skip daytime contacts on 40 meters, a horizontal dipole placed between 1/10 and 1/4 wavelength above ground — low enough that it fires most of its energy straight up rather than toward the horizon — is the most effective near-vertical-incidence-skywave antenna, putting a strong signal down into your own regional "skip zone" that a higher antenna would fly right over.

End-fed half-wave

Feeding a half-wave dipole right at one end (rather than the center) lands on a very high feed point impedance — the same "impedance rises toward the ends" rule from G9B, taken to its extreme at the very tip — which is why an end-fed half-wave needs a dedicated high-impedance matching unit that a center-fed dipole never needs.

A log-periodic antenna looks superficially Yagi-like but is built on a different principle: element length and spacing vary logarithmically along its boom, rather than being tuned around one specific frequency. That's exactly why its headline advantage is wide bandwidth: a log-periodic keeps working reasonably well across a large multiple of frequencies precisely because it doesn't depend on a single resonant length the way a Yagi's driven element does — the tradeoff is that per-element gain is lower than a comparable Yagi's.

A "halo" antenna (a folded dipole bent into a loop, common on VHF) radiates omnidirectionally in the plane of the halo — the bend trades away the ordinary dipole figure-eight for all-around coverage in one plane, at the cost of some size and simplicity. An electrically small loop antenna (less than 1/10 wavelength in circumference — a specialized receiving antenna, distinct from a full-size resonant loop) has nulls in its pattern broadside to the loop — directly out of its face, rather than in the plane of the wire itself — which is the opposite null direction from what its small size might suggest.

A Beverage antenna is a long, low horizontal wire used for directional receiving on the MF and low HF bands — a receive-only specialist, not built or used for transmitting. Antenna traps (parallel LC circuits inserted into an element) exist to enable multiband operation from a single physical antenna, switching part of the element in or out of the circuit depending on frequency — the direct practical cost of that convenience is poor harmonic rejection compared to a single-band antenna, since a multiband design that's resonant on several unrelated frequencies is, by the same token, less selective about rejecting any one of them.

A "screwdriver" mobile antenna adjusts its feed point impedance by varying its base loading inductance — a small motor inside the base physically moves a coil tap or slides a coil in and out, remotely retuning the antenna's electrical length without anyone touching the whip itself. And the common name for a dipole with a single central support (the feed line's support point doubling as the antenna's only mast, with both ends drooping down and away) is an Inverted V — a popular, easy-to-erect variation requiring just one support instead of two.

Finally, one more antenna-stacking fact that follows directly from G9C's dBi/dBd and stacking-gain material: vertically stacking horizontally polarized Yagi antennas doesn't just add gain — its main practical advantage is that it narrows the main lobe in elevation, concentrating radiated energy at a lower, more useful takeoff angle for long-distance work instead of wasting some of it higher in the sky.
